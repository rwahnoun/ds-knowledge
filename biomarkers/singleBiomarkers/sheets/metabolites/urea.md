# [[[[urea]]|Urea]]

The most abundant organic solute in human urine (~25–50% of all urinary solids). Key marker for nitrogen balance, renal function, and protein catabolism. Has strong, well-characterised FTIR and Raman signatures. See [[signatures]], [[optical-properties]], and [[signal-processing]] for spectral detection context.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> [[[[urea]]|Urea]] dominates the FTIR and Raman spectra of urine. Its C-N asymmetric stretch at ~1468 cm⁻¹ (FTIR) and C-N symmetric stretch at 1003 cm⁻¹ (Raman) are the primary quantification peaks. Hydration state affects the FTIR band position; four spectral components are needed for accurate decomposition in dried urine films.

Structural formula: `H₂N—C(=O)—NH₂`

[[[[urea]]|Urea]] is the simplest diamide of carbonic acid — a small, highly polar, uncharged molecule with strong hydrogen-bonding capacity. At physiological urinary pH (4.5–8.0), it does not ionise (pKa ~0.1 for carbonyl protonation).

## Medical Information

### Origin — The [[[[urea]]|Urea]] Cycle

**Endogenous:** Synthesised almost exclusively in the **liver** (periportal hepatocytes) via the [[urea]] cycle (Krebs-Henseleit cycle, 1932). Converts toxic ammonia (from amino acid deamination) into non-toxic, water-soluble [[urea]].

**Steps:**
1. Carbamoyl [[phosphate]] synthesis (mitochondrial, rate-limiting): NH₃ + CO₂ + 2 ATP → Carbamoyl [[phosphate]]; catalysed by CPS I (activated by N-acetylglutamate).
2. Citrulline formation (mitochondrial): Carbamoyl [[phosphate]] + Ornithine → Citrulline; catalysed by OTC.
3. Argininosuccinate synthesis (cytoplasmic): Citrulline + Aspartate + ATP → Argininosuccinate; catalysed by ASS.
4. Arginine formation: Argininosuccinate → Arginine + Fumarate; catalysed by ASL.
5. [[[[urea]]|Urea]] release: Arginine + H₂O → [[[[urea]]|Urea]] + Ornithine; catalysed by arginase.

**Net reaction:** 2 NH₃ + CO₂ + 3 ATP + H₂O → [[[[urea]]|Urea]] + 2 ADP + AMP + 4 Pᵢ + PPᵢ

Production: ~10–20 g/day depending on dietary protein intake.

**Exogenous:** Not a dietary nutrient. Production is driven by dietary protein intake. Present in trace amounts in some foods; used as a topical humectant in skin products.

### Biological Roles

- **Primary:** Nitrogen waste disposal — ~80–90% of nitrogen excretion occurs as urinary [[urea]].
- **Renal concentrating mechanism:** Contributes significantly to the medullary osmotic gradient. [[[[urea]]|Urea]] transporters (UT-A1, UT-A2, UT-B) allow [[urea]] recycling in the countercurrent system.
- **Skin hydration:** Component of the Natural Moisturising Factor (NMF) in stratum corneum.
- **Protein denaturant (high concentrations):** 6–8 M [[urea]] disrupts hydrogen bonds; used in biochemistry, not physiological.

### Elimination Pathway

Freely filtered at glomerulus (100%). ~40–60% passively reabsorbed in proximal tubule and thin descending limb. In inner medullary collecting duct, ADH regulates UT-A1 for countercurrent [[urea]] recycling. Remaining ~40–60% excreted in urine. Minor GI (~15–40% of produced [[urea]]; hydrolysed by bacterial urease → NH₃ → reabsorbed or excreted). Minor sweat losses (~1–2 g/day).

### Clinical Levels

| Compartment | Reference Range |
|---|---|
| **Serum/Plasma [[urea]]** | 2.5–7.1 mmol/L (15–43 mg/dL) in adults |
| **BUN** | 7–20 mg/dL (adults 18–59 y) |
| **BUN (>60 y)** | 8–23 mg/dL |
| **Urinary [[urea]]** | 12–20 g/24 h (200–333 mmol/24 h) |
| **Urinary [[urea]] concentration** | 200–400 mmol/L (variable with hydration) |

### Factors Influencing Levels

**Increased [[urea]]:** High protein diet, GI bleeding, catabolic states (fever, burns, sepsis), dehydration, renal impairment (reduced GFR), post-renal obstruction.

**Decreased [[urea]]:** Low protein diet/malnutrition, severe liver disease (impaired [[urea]] cycle), overhydration/SIADH, pregnancy, anabolic states, [[urea]] cycle inborn errors.

### Associated Pathologies

| Condition | [[[[urea]]\|Urea]]/BUN Level | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Azotemia (prerenal)** | Elevated BUN, BUN/[[creatinin\|Creatinine]] >20:1 | Dehydration, hypotension, oliguria |
| **Azotemia (renal/intrinsic)** | Elevated BUN, BUN/[[creatinin\|Creatinine]] ~10–15:1 | Oedema, hypertension, haematuria |
| **Azotemia (post-renal)** | Elevated BUN | Obstruction, anuria |
| **Uraemia** | Markedly elevated (BUN >60 mg/dL) | Nausea, pericarditis, encephalopathy, uraemic frost |
| **Liver failure/cirrhosis** | Low [[urea]] | Jaundice, coagulopathy, hepatic encephalopathy |
| **[[[[urea]]\|Urea]] cycle disorders** | Low [[urea]], hyperammonaemia | Neonatal encephalopathy, seizures; ~1:35,000 births |

### Presence in Urine

The **most abundant organic solute** in human urine (~25–50% of all urinary solids). Normal excretion 12–20 g/day. Concentration 200–400 mmol/L (highly variable). Never causes crystalluria (solubility ~1080 g/L at 20 °C). Form: native, dissolved, uncharged molecule CO(NH₂)₂.

## Detection in Urine

### Clinical Assays

| Method | Principle | Detection | LOD | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Urease-GLDH (gold standard)** | Urease: [[urea]] → 2 NH₄⁺ + CO₂; GLDH: NH₄⁺ + α-KG + [[nadh\|NADH]] → L-Glu + NAD⁺ | 340 nm ([[nadh\|NADH]] decrease) | ~0.1 mM | All major platforms (Roche Cobas, Siemens, Abbott); CV <3%; kinetic mode |
| **Diacetyl monoxime (DAM)** | [[[[urea]]\|Urea]] + diacetyl (from DAM, acid + heat) → pink-red diazine | 540 nm | ~0.5 mM | Simple/low-cost; toxic reagents; less specific |
| **Berthelot (indophenol)** | Urease → NH₃ + phenol + hypochlorite → indophenol (blue) | ~630 nm | ~0.2 mM | Historical; still used in some labs |
| **Conductimetric** | Urease converts uncharged [[urea]] to ionic products → conductivity increase | Conductivity | ~0.5 mM | Less common |

Gold standard: **Enzymatic urease-GLDH kinetic UV assay** (340 nm, [[nadh|NADH]]).

Optimal specimen: **24-hour urine** for nitrogen balance. Spot urine with [[creatinin|creatinine]] correction for screening. Preserve at 2–8 °C or with thymol/boric acid to inhibit bacterial urease.

### Interferences

| Interference | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Ammonia contamination** | False elevation | Ammonia is the measured intermediate |
| **Bacterial contamination** | False decrease | Bacterial urease degrades [[urea]] |
| **Fluoride (NaF)** | False decrease | Inhibits urease |
| **Haemolysis** | Variable | Releases intracellular ammonium |
| **Lipaemia** | Positive bias | Light scattering at 340 nm |
| **DAM method — thiourea, guanidine** | False elevation | React with diacetyl to form similar chromophores |

### Spectroscopic Detection

**UV-Vis:** [[[[urea]]|Urea]] has **no significant chromophore** (no useful absorption 200–800 nm). All clinical assays rely on coupled reactions to generate a measurable chromophore.

**NIR:** [[[[urea]]|Urea]] absorbs via overtone/combination bands of N-H and C=O:
- **~2150 nm** (combination: NH₂ deformation + C=O stretch)
- **~1500 nm** (first overtone of N-H stretch)
- LOD ~1–5 mM in aqueous solution; adequate for urinary concentrations (200–400 mM)
- PLS regression with fibre-optic probes; no sample prep

**Raman:** [[[[urea]]|Urea]] has **characteristic peaks** allowing direct detection:

| Peak (cm⁻¹) | Assignment |
|---|---|
| **1003** | C-N symmetric stretch (νs) — primary quantification peak |
| **1540** | NH₂ bending (δ) |
| **~1640** | C=O stretch (ν) |
| **~3340–3440** | N-H stretches |

- Conventional Raman: LOD ~5–10 mM in aqueous solution; adequate for clinical urine.
- SERS (Fe₃O₄@C@Ag): LOD = 5.68 nM; enhancement factor 3.67 × 10⁶ (RSC Advances 2025).
- Excitation: 532, 633, or 785 nm.

**FTIR:** Strong, well-characterised bands:

| Band (cm⁻¹) | Assignment | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **~1627–1640** | C=O stretching | Overlaps with water bending (~1640 cm⁻¹) |
| **~1585–1600** | NH₂ bending | Asymmetric deformation |
| **~1460–1468** | C-N antisymmetric stretch (νas) | **Most diagnostic band; shifts with hydration: 1468 cm⁻¹ (solution) → 1443–1464 cm⁻¹ (dried)** |
| **~3340, 3440** | N-H stretching | Symmetric and antisymmetric |

ATR-FTIR preferred for urine analysis. Shaw et al. (2000) demonstrated reagent-free quantification of [[urea]], [[creatinin|creatinine]], and total protein from dried urine films. Rich & Iwaki (2017): four components needed for accurate νas(CN) decomposition in dried urine. [[[[urea]]|Urea]] is the **dominant organic solute** → strong IR signal well above detection limits.

**Fluorescence:** [[[[urea]]|Urea]] is **non-fluorescent**. All fluorescence methods use indirect detection via urease-coupled pH-sensitive probes (fluorescein Ex 490 / Em 520 nm, LOD 0.1–1 mM) or quantum dot systems (N-CDs: Ex 360 / Em 440 nm, LOD ~0.05 mM).

**Voltammetry:**
- **Enzyme-based (urease):** Immobilised urease; amperometric/potentiometric/conductimetric transduction. LOD 10–100 µM. Linear range 0.1–100 mM.
- **Non-enzymatic Ni-based:** Ni²⁺/Ni³⁺ redox catalyses [[urea]] oxidation in alkaline media. NiO nanostructures: LOD 10 µM; Ni(bzimpy)₂: LOD 1.5 µM.

### Detection Methods Comparison

| Method | LOD | Key Parameter | Sample Prep | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Urease-GLDH (gold standard)** | ~0.1 mM | 340 nm ([[nadh\|NADH]]) | Dilution | Automated, precise, specific | Reagent cost |
| **DAM colorimetric** | ~0.5 mM | 540 nm | Acid + heat | Simple, low cost | Toxic reagents, less specific |
| **NIR** | ~1–5 mM | 1500 + 2150 nm | None | Reagent-free, non-destructive | Chemometric calibration required |
| **ATR-FTIR** | N/A (dominant) | 1468 cm⁻¹ (νas CN) | None or drying | Reagent-free, multianalyte | Water overlap; hydration artefacts |
| **Raman (conventional)** | ~5–10 mM | 1003 cm⁻¹ | None | Non-destructive | Low sensitivity |
| **SERS** | ~5.7 nM | 1003 cm⁻¹ | Nanoparticle mixing | Ultra-sensitive | Substrate cost, reproducibility |
| **Fluorescence (QD/enzyme)** | 0.01–1 mM | Ex 320–490 / Em 430–580 nm | Urease + buffer | Sensitive, versatile | Indirect; enzyme stability |
| **Ni-electrode voltammetry** | 1.5–10 µM | ~+0.5 V vs Ag/AgCl | Alkaline pH adjustment | Enzyme-free | Requires alkaline media |
| **Urease amperometric** | 10–100 µM | Applied potential varies | pH buffer | Specific | Enzyme degradation |

## Gaps

- Hydration-state artefacts in dried urine FTIR complicate [[urea]] quantification; validated four-component decomposition models needed for clinical platforms
- NIR-based [[urea]] in urine requires population-representative PLS calibration; existing models may not generalise across disease states
- SERS ultra-sensitivity (5.7 nM) is well beyond clinical needs — reproducibility of SERS substrates in a point-of-care device is the limiting factor
- [[[[urea]]|Urea]] in the renal concentrating mechanism interacts with ADH and aquaporins; urinary [[urea]]-to-osmolality ratio as a renal function index is underexplored
