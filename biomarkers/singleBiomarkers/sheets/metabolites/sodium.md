# [[[[sodium]]|Sodium]]

The principal extracellular cation. Urinary [[sodium]] reflects dietary intake and renal volume regulation. Used for FENa (AKI aetiology), SIADH diagnosis, and dietary salt assessment. No useful spectroscopic signature in UV-Vis, NIR, or Raman — ISE is the only practical measurement approach.

[[[[sodium]]|Sodium]] is the **principal extracellular cation**, critical for fluid balance, nerve conduction, and muscle contraction. In urine, [[sodium]] reflects dietary intake and renal handling, serving as a key diagnostic marker for volume status, AKI aetiology (FENa), and electrolyte disorders.

### Molecules Not to Be Confused With

| Molecule | Formula | MW (g/mol) | Key Difference |

## Medical Information

### Origin

**Endogenous:** Not synthesised. Total body [[sodium]] ~60 mmol/kg (~4,200 mmol in 70 kg adult). ~40% in bone, 55% in ECF, 5% in ICF.

**Exogenous:** Sole source is dietary [[sodium]] [[chloride]] (NaCl). Typical Western diet: 100–250 mmol/day. WHO recommendation: <2 g Na/day (<5 g NaCl/day).

### Biological Roles

- **Extracellular fluid osmolality regulation:** Na⁺ is the main determinant of ECF osmolality (~275–295 mOsm/kg), controlling water distribution between compartments.
- **Nerve conduction and muscle contraction:** Na⁺ influx through voltage-gated channels generates action potentials.
- **Acid-base balance:** Na⁺/H⁺ exchange in renal tubules contributes to acid excretion.
- **Nutrient co-transport:** Na⁺-dependent transporters (SGLT for [[glucose]], amino acid transporters).
- **Blood pressure regulation:** [[[[sodium]]|Sodium]] balance directly affects blood volume and BP.

### Elimination Pathway

Freely filtered at glomerulus (~25,200 mmol/day). **~99% reabsorbed:** 67% proximal tubule, 25% thick ascending loop (NKCC2), 5% distal tubule (NCC), 2–3% collecting duct (ENaC, aldosterone-regulated). Final urinary excretion matches dietary intake at steady state.

### Clinical Levels

| Compartment | Reference Range |
|---|---|
| **Serum [[sodium]]** | 135–145 mmol/L |
| **Urinary [[sodium]] (24-h)** | 40–220 mmol/day (diet-dependent) |
| **Urinary [[sodium]] (spot)** | 20–200 mmol/L (highly variable) |
| **FENa** | <1% in prerenal AKI; >2% in intrinsic renal AKI |

### Factors Influencing Levels

**Increased urinary Na:** High dietary salt, diuretics, salt-wasting nephropathy, adrenal insufficiency, SIADH, ATN (FENa >2%), post-obstructive diuresis.

**Decreased urinary Na:** Low salt diet, volume depletion (FENa <1%), heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, hyperaldosteronism.

### Associated Pathologies

| Condition | Urine Na Pattern | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Prerenal AKI** | UNa <20 mmol/L, FENa <1% | Oliguria, tachycardia, dry mucosa |
| **Intrinsic renal AKI (ATN)** | UNa >40 mmol/L, FENa >2% | Oliguria/anuria, rising [[creatinin\|creatinine]] |
| **SIADH** | UNa >30 mmol/L, dilute serum | Hyponatraemia, confusion, seizures |
| **Hyperaldosteronism** | Low UNa despite hypertension | Hypertension, hypokalaemia |
| **Salt-wasting nephropathy** | Inappropriately high UNa | Hyponatraemia, polyuria |

### Presence in Urine

Normally present. Urinary Na excretion is the primary mechanism for Na balance. Form: **Na⁺ ion** — fully dissociated, hydrated monovalent cation. NaCl is highly soluble (~360 g/L); [[sodium]] does not form urinary crystals.

## Detection in Urine

### Clinical Assays

| Method | Principle | LOD | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ion-selective electrode (ISE) (gold standard)** | Na⁺-selective glass or polymer membrane; Nernstian potential | ~1 mmol/L | Standard on all automated analysers; CV <2%; direct ISE preferred for urine |
| **Flame emission photometry** | Na atoms in flame emit at 589 nm | ~0.01 mmol/L | Historical reference; largely replaced by ISE |
| **Flame AAS** | Ground-state Na atoms absorb at 589 nm | ~0.01 mmol/L | High sensitivity; specialised equipment |

Gold standard: **Direct ISE** on automated chemistry platforms (Roche Cobas, Siemens Advia, Abbott Architect). Reference method: flame emission photometry at 589 nm.

Optimal specimen: **24-hour urine** for daily Na excretion; **spot urine** with [[creatinin|creatinine]] correction for FENa and acute decisions. No special preservatives. Stable at room temperature for days.

### Interferences

| Interference | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Very high K⁺** | Minor Na⁺ ISE interference | Cross-selectivity of membrane |
| **Surfactant contamination** | Variable | Alters electrode membrane response |
| **Lithium (high levels)** | Minor positive bias | Some ISE cross-react with Li⁺ |

### Spectroscopic Detection

**Flame emission (589 nm):** The [[sodium]] D-line at 589.0 and 589.6 nm. LOD ~0.01 mmol/L.

**UV-Vis / NIR:** Na⁺ as a simple monatomic ion has **no absorption in UV-Vis or NIR**. Not detectable by molecular spectroscopy.

**Raman:** Na⁺ has **no Raman-active vibrational modes** (monatomic ion). Cannot be detected by Raman.

**FTIR:** Na⁺ has **no infrared absorption** (no molecular bonds). Lattice modes of NaCl appear only in far-IR (<200 cm⁻¹) in crystalline state — not applicable to solution analysis.

**Voltammetry (ISE):** Na⁺-selective glass or polymer (bis(12-crown-4)) membrane ISE. LOD ~1 mmol/L. Na⁺ reduction potential (E₀ = −2.71 V vs SHE) is too negative for aqueous voltammetry.

### Detection Methods Comparison

| Method | LOD | Key Parameter | Sample Prep | Strengths | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **ISE (direct)** | ~1 mmol/L | Potentiometric | None/dilution | Standard, fast, precise | Electrode maintenance |
| **Flame emission** | ~0.01 mmol/L | 589 nm emission | Dilution | Reference, multi-element | Gas flame needed |
| **FAAS** | ~0.01 mmol/L | 589 nm absorption | Dilution | High sensitivity | Specialised equipment |
| **SBFI fluorescence** | ~0.1 mmol/L | Ex 340-380 / Em 505 nm | Probe addition | Imaging-capable | Narrow dynamic range |
| **CoroNa Green** | ~1 mmol/L | Ex 492 / Em 516 nm | Probe addition | Simple | Limited range |
| **ICP-OES** | ~0.001 mmol/L | 589 nm emission | Dilution | Multi-element | Instrument cost |
| **Raman / FTIR** | N/A | N/A | N/A | Not applicable | No signal |

## Gaps

- No spectroscopic (Raman, FTIR, NIR, UV-Vis) method for urinary Na — ion-selective electrochemistry is the only viable approach beyond flame photometry
- Spot urine Na with [[creatinin|creatinine]] correction is widely used but not validated against 24-h collections in all patient populations
- SBFI fluorescence has inadequate dynamic range for the full urinary Na concentration range (20–200 mmol/L) without serial dilution
- Paper-based ISE sensors for point-of-care Na measurement remain prototype-stage
