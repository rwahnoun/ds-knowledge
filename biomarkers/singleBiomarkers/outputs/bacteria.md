# Bacteria

**Author:** Usense Healthcare

## Identity Sheet

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Name** | Bacteria (Bacteriuria) |
| **Other names** | Urinary bacteria, bacteriuria, urinary tract pathogens |
| **Nature** | Prokaryotic microorganisms (not a single molecule) |
| **Size** | Typically 0.5–5 µm (cocci ~1 µm; rods 1–3 µm length) |
| **Common species in UTI** | *Escherichia coli* (75–90%), *Klebsiella pneumoniae*, *Proteus mirabilis*, *Enterococcus faecalis*, *Staphylococcus saprophyticus* |
| **Diagnostic threshold** | >=10^5 CFU/mL (significant bacteriuria); >=10^3 CFU/mL in symptomatic women |

**Representative morphologies:**

```
  Cocci (spheres):    o o o     (e.g. Staphylococcus, Enterococcus)

  Bacilli (rods):    [===]      (e.g. E. coli, Klebsiella, Proteus)

  Chains:            o-o-o-o   (e.g. Streptococcus)
```

Bacteria in urine represent a biomarker category rather than a single chemical entity. The presence and quantity of bacteria in urine (bacteriuria) is a key diagnostic marker for urinary tract infections (UTIs). Bacteria are detected by growth (culture), biochemical by-products (nitrite from nitrate reduction), enzyme activity (catalase, oxidase), or direct visualisation/counting.

### Molecules Not to Be Confused With

| Entity | Description | Key Difference |
|---|---|---|
| **Yeast / Fungi** | *Candida* spp., budding oval cells 3–8 µm | Eukaryotic; larger; may indicate fungal UTI or contamination |
| **White blood cells (WBCs)** | Leukocytes, 10–15 µm | Much larger; indicate host immune response, not organisms |
| **Epithelial cells** | Squamous cells, 40–60 µm | Very large, flat; indicate contamination if numerous |
| **Amorphous debris** | Precipitated salts/crystals | Non-biological; no metabolic activity |

## Medical Information

### Origin

#### Endogenous

The urinary tract is normally **sterile** above the distal urethra. The distal urethra harbours commensal flora (lactobacilli, coagulase-negative staphylococci, corynebacteria). Bacteriuria arises when:
- Periurethral organisms ascend into the bladder (most common route, especially in women)
- Haematogenous seeding (rare; e.g., *Staphylococcus aureus* bacteraemia)
- Lymphatic spread (rare)
- Direct inoculation (catheterisation, instrumentation)

The most common pathogen, *E. coli*, originates from the patient's own gastrointestinal flora.

#### Exogenous

- **Healthcare-associated UTIs:** Catheter-associated UTI (CAUTI) from hospital environment organisms (multi-drug resistant *Klebsiella*, *Pseudomonas*, *Enterobacter*)
- **Sexually transmitted pathogens:** *Chlamydia trachomatis*, *Neisseria gonorrhoeae* (urethritis, not classic UTI)
- **Environmental waterborne organisms:** Rare (e.g., *Leptospira*)

### Primary & Secondary Biological Roles

**Primary role (in host context):**
- **Pathogen indicator:** Bacteria in urine above threshold concentrations indicate infection requiring treatment.

**Secondary roles:**
- **Urinary microbiome research:** Emerging evidence of a low-biomass urinary microbiome even in healthy individuals (detected by enhanced culture and metagenomics), though clinical significance is uncertain.
- **Nitrite production:** Gram-negative bacteria reduce urinary nitrate to nitrite — the basis for the dipstick nitrite test.

### Catabolism and Elimination Pathway

- The healthy urinary tract eliminates bacteria through **voiding** (mechanical washout), **antimicrobial peptides** (defensins, cathelicidins), **urothelial mucus layer** (GAG layer), **Tamm-Horsfall protein** (uromodulin), and **immune surveillance** (neutrophils, macrophages).
- In infection, the host mounts an inflammatory response: neutrophil influx (pyuria), cytokine release, complement activation.
- Antibiotics are the primary therapeutic elimination pathway.

### Expression in Humans

#### Normal Levels

| Compartment | Reference Range |
|---|---|
| **Mid-stream urine (MSU)** | <10^3 CFU/mL (culture-negative / insignificant) |
| **Catheter specimen** | <10^2 CFU/mL considered negative |
| **Suprapubic aspirate** | Any growth is significant |

#### Factors Influencing Levels

**Increased bacterial count:**
- Female sex (short urethra)
- Sexual activity
- Urinary catheterisation
- Urinary obstruction (stones, BPH)
- Diabetes mellitus
- Immunosuppression
- Pregnancy
- Poor hygiene / contaminated collection

**Decreased / absent:**
- Recent antibiotic therapy
- High urine flow rate (dilution)
- Acidic urine pH
- Adequate voiding habits
- Antimicrobial properties of urine (urea, organic acids)

#### Associated Pathologies

| Condition | Bacterial Level | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Uncomplicated cystitis** | >=10^3 CFU/mL (*E. coli*) in symptomatic women | Dysuria, frequency, urgency; prevalence ~50–60% of women lifetime |
| **Pyelonephritis** | >=10^5 CFU/mL + pyuria | Fever, flank pain, nausea, costovertebral angle tenderness |
| **Asymptomatic bacteriuria (ASB)** | >=10^5 CFU/mL, no symptoms | No symptoms; significant in pregnancy (~2–10% prevalence) and pre-surgery |
| **Catheter-associated UTI (CAUTI)** | >=10^3 CFU/mL + symptoms | Fever, suprapubic pain; ~80% of nosocomial UTIs |
| **Urosepsis** | Positive blood + urine culture | Systemic sepsis from urinary source; ~25% of all sepsis |

### Presence in Urine

**Should it be normally present?** **No** — the bladder urine is normally sterile. Low-level commensal colonisation of the distal urethra means very small numbers (<10^3 CFU/mL) may appear in voided specimens without indicating infection.

**Normal levels:** <10^3 CFU/mL in clean-catch midstream urine.

**Form in urine:** Intact, viable (or non-viable) bacterial cells — planktonic (free-floating) or occasionally in biofilms (catheter surfaces).

**Pathological significance:**

| Finding | Possible Causes | Prevalence |
|---|---|---|
| **>=10^5 CFU/mL** | UTI (cystitis, pyelonephritis), ASB | UTI: ~150 million cases/year globally |
| **10^3–10^5 CFU/mL** | Early/treated UTI, contamination, urethritis | Context-dependent |
| **<10^3 CFU/mL** | Normal / contamination | — |

**Solubility:** Not applicable (cellular organisms, not dissolved molecules).

## Detection in Urine

### Available Clinical Assays

1. **Urine culture (gold standard):**
   - **Principle:** Urine plated on agar media (blood agar, MacConkey, CLED); incubated 18–24 h at 37 °C; colonies counted and identified.
   - **Detection:** Colony-forming units per mL (CFU/mL).
   - **LOD:** ~10^2 CFU/mL (standard inoculum loop technique).
   - **Advantages:** Definitive identification + antibiotic susceptibility testing.
   - **Disadvantages:** 18–48 h turnaround; labour-intensive.

2. **Dipstick nitrite test:**
   - **Principle:** Griess reaction — bacterial nitrate reductase converts urinary nitrate to nitrite; nitrite reacts with aromatic amine + coupling agent → pink azo dye.
   - **Detection:** Visual colour change on reagent pad.
   - **LOD:** ~10^5 CFU/mL (requires sufficient bacteria and incubation time in bladder).
   - **Sensitivity:** 45–60%; **Specificity:** 85–98%.
   - **Advantages:** Rapid (<2 min), inexpensive.
   - **Disadvantages:** Misses non-nitrate-reducing organisms (Enterococcus, Staphylococcus); requires bladder dwell time >=4 h.

3. **Dipstick leukocyte esterase (LE):**
   - **Principle:** Detects esterase from neutrophils (indirect marker of infection).
   - **Detection:** Indoxyl ester substrate → indigo colour.
   - **Sensitivity:** 75–90% for UTI.
   - **Not bacteria-specific** — detects host inflammatory response.

4. **Automated flow cytometry (Sysmex UF-5000, Iris iQ200):**
   - **Principle:** Fluorescent labelling of bacterial nucleic acid + flow cytometry counting.
   - **Detection:** Bacteria/µL count; fluorescent scatterplot.
   - **LOD:** ~10–100 bacteria/µL (~10^4–10^5/mL).
   - **Advantages:** Rapid (~1 min), automated, differentiates rods vs cocci.
   - **Disadvantages:** Cannot identify species or provide susceptibility.

5. **Gram stain microscopy:**
   - **Principle:** Centrifuged urine sediment stained with crystal violet/iodine; examined microscopically.
   - **Detection:** Visual identification of bacteria morphology and Gram reaction.
   - **LOD:** ~10^4–10^5 CFU/mL (>=1 organism per oil-immersion field in uncentrifuged urine ≈ 10^5 CFU/mL).
   - **Advantages:** Rapid, guides empirical therapy.
   - **Disadvantages:** Operator-dependent, low sensitivity.

### Optimal Urine Type for Measurement

- **Clean-catch midstream urine (MSU):** Standard for culture; reduces periurethral contamination.
- **First morning urine:** Higher bacterial concentration (longer bladder dwell time >=4 h); preferred for nitrite testing.
- **Catheter specimen:** Preferred for catheterised patients.
- **Suprapubic aspirate:** Gold standard specimen (bypasses urethra) — any growth significant.
- **Transport:** Process within **2 hours** or refrigerate (4 °C) to prevent bacterial multiplication. Boric acid preservative tubes maintain counts for up to 24 h.

### Actual Gold Standard

**Quantitative urine culture** on standard media (blood agar + MacConkey or CLED agar) with colony counting and species identification (MALDI-TOF MS, biochemical panels, or molecular methods). Threshold for significance: >=10^5 CFU/mL (Kass criteria), though >=10^3 CFU/mL is accepted for symptomatic women with pyuria. Antibiotic susceptibility testing (AST) by disc diffusion or MIC determination is integral.

### Interferences in Measurement

| Interference | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Periurethral contamination** | False positive | Mixed flora from distal urethra/skin |
| **Recent antibiotics** | False negative (culture) | Suppress bacterial growth |
| **High urine flow / dilution** | False negative (nitrite, culture) | Bacteria diluted below threshold |
| **Short bladder dwell time** | False negative (nitrite) | Insufficient time for nitrate reduction |
| **Non-nitrate-reducing organisms** | False negative (nitrite) | Enterococcus, Staphylococcus, Candida do not produce nitrite |
| **High ascorbic acid** | False negative (dipstick) | Reduces diazo coupling reaction |
| **Boric acid preservative** | May inhibit some organisms | Bacteriostatic; excessive amounts kill bacteria |

### Research Detection Methods

#### Spectroscopy Detection (UV-Vis / NIR)

- **UV-Vis turbidimetry:** Bacterial growth measured by optical density (OD) at **600 nm** or **550 nm** — standard microbiological technique. OD600 = 0.1 ≈ 10^8 CFU/mL. LOD: ~10^6 CFU/mL. Too insensitive for direct urine use.
- **NIR spectroscopy:** Some studies use NIR (700–2500 nm) with chemometrics to classify bacterial species in pure cultures. Not validated for urine matrix. LOD: ~10^6–10^7 CFU/mL.
- **Sample prep:** None (direct measurement of liquid).

#### Fluorescence Detection

- **Nucleic acid staining + flow cytometry (Sysmex UF):** Polymethine/oxazine dyes (proprietary) bind bacterial DNA/RNA. Ex 488 nm / Em 530 nm (green) and Em >600 nm (red). LOD: ~10–100 bacteria/µL.
- **FISH (Fluorescence In Situ Hybridisation):** Species-specific 16S rRNA probes labelled with fluorophores (FITC: Ex 490 / Em 520 nm; Cy3: Ex 550 / Em 570 nm). LOD: ~10^3 CFU/mL. Sample prep: fixation, hybridisation (2–4 h).
- **Calcein-AM viability staining:** Live bacteria convert non-fluorescent calcein-AM to fluorescent calcein (Ex 495 / Em 515 nm). LOD: ~10^3 CFU/mL.
- **Quantum dot conjugates:** QD-antibody conjugates for pathogen-specific fluorescent detection. Ex 350–405 nm / Em 525–655 nm (size-dependent). LOD: ~10^2 CFU/mL in buffer, ~10^3 in urine.

#### Raman Detection

Raman spectroscopy provides species-specific fingerprints of bacteria:

| Peak (cm⁻¹) | Assignment |
|---|---|
| **~720–730** | Adenine (nucleic acids) |
| **~780–790** | Cytosine/uracil ring breathing |
| **~1003** | Phenylalanine ring breathing |
| **~1240–1260** | Amide III (proteins) |
| **~1450** | CH₂ deformation (lipids/proteins) |
| **~1660** | Amide I (proteins) |

- **Conventional Raman:** Single-cell identification possible with confocal Raman (532 or 785 nm laser). LOD: single cell (but requires capture/concentration from urine).
- **SERS:** Silver/gold nanoparticles enable detection in complex matrices; LOD ~10^2 CFU/mL with SERS-based assays.
- **Sample prep:** Centrifugation/filtration to concentrate bacteria; transfer to optical substrate.

#### FTIR Detection

Bacterial FTIR fingerprints:

| Band (cm⁻¹) | Assignment | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **~1080** | PO₂⁻ symmetric stretch (nucleic acids, phospholipids) | Species-variable |
| **~1240** | PO₂⁻ asymmetric stretch | — |
| **~1540** | Amide II (proteins) | — |
| **~1650** | Amide I (proteins) | — |
| **~2850–2960** | C–H stretches (lipids) | Membrane lipids |
| **~3300** | N–H / O–H stretch (proteins, water) | — |

- ATR-FTIR with multivariate analysis can classify bacterial species from pure isolates (accuracy >90%). Direct detection in urine requires concentration (filtration). LOD: ~10^5 CFU/mL without concentration.
- **Sample prep:** Centrifugation, washing, drying on ATR crystal.

#### Voltammetry Detection

- **Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS):** Interdigitated electrodes measure impedance changes as bacteria grow in urine. LOD: ~10^3–10^4 CFU/mL in 1–3 h.
- **Amperometric biosensors:** Antibody- or aptamer-functionalised electrodes with enzymatic signal amplification. LOD: ~10^2 CFU/mL.
- **Differential pulse voltammetry (DPV):** Direct oxidation of bacterial components at carbon electrodes. LOD: ~10^4 CFU/mL.
- **Sample prep:** Minimal; direct urine application.

### Other Detection Technologies

1. **MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry:** Direct from positive cultures for rapid species ID (<1 h after growth). Sample prep: colony pick + matrix application. LOD: requires visible colony.

2. **Molecular diagnostics (PCR/LAMP):** 16S rRNA gene PCR or species-specific primers. LOD: ~10–100 CFU/mL. Turnaround: 1–4 h. Sample prep: DNA extraction.

3. **Lateral flow immunoassays:** Antibody-based strips for specific pathogens (e.g., *E. coli*). LOD: ~10^4–10^5 CFU/mL. No instrument needed.

4. **Microfluidic/lab-on-chip:** Integrated concentration + detection (impedance, fluorescence, or optical). LOD: ~10–10^3 CFU/mL. Sample prep: automated on-chip.

5. **ATP bioluminescence:** Bacterial ATP detected by luciferin-luciferase. LOD: ~10^3–10^4 CFU/mL. Rapid (<5 min). Non-specific (host cell ATP interferes).
