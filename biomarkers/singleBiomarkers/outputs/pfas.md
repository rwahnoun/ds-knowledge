# PFAs (Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances)

**Author:** Usense Healthcare

---

## Identity Sheet

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Name** | Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances (PFAS) |
| **Other names** | Forever chemicals, PFCs, PFOS, PFOA, GenX |
| **Chemical formula** | Variable; general: CnF(2n+1)-R where R = functional group |
| **Key species** | PFOS (C₈HF₁₇O₃S, MW 500.13); PFOA (C₈HF₁₅O₂, MW 414.07) |
| **CAS numbers** | PFOS: 1763-23-1; PFOA: 335-67-1 |
| **Nature** | Class of >12,000 synthetic fluorinated organic compounds |
| **Solubility** | Variable (amphiphilic); PFOA ~3.4 g/L; PFOS ~0.57 g/L |

**General structural formula:**

```
  F F F F F F F F
  | | | | | | | |
  C-C-C-C-C-C-C-C-SO3(-)    (PFOS: perfluorooctane sulfonate)
  | | | | | | | |
  F F F F F F F F
```

PFAS are a large class of synthetic organofluorine compounds characterised by strong C-F bonds that make them extremely persistent in the environment ("forever chemicals"). They are used in non-stick coatings (Teflon), firefighting foams (AFFF), waterproof textiles, food packaging, and industrial processes. Due to their extreme persistence, PFAS are ubiquitous environmental contaminants detected in the blood and urine of virtually the entire global population. Urinary PFAS are emerging biomonitoring targets.

### Molecules Not to Be Confused With

| Molecule | Formula | MW (g/mol) | Key Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PFOS** | C₈F₁₇SO₃⁻ | 499.12 | Perfluorooctane sulfonate (long-chain, legacy; half-life ~5 years in humans) |
| **PFOA** | C₇F₁₅COOH | 414.07 | Perfluorooctanoic acid (long-chain, legacy; half-life ~3.5 years) |
| **GenX (HFPO-DA)** | C₆HF₁₁O₃ | 330.05 | Short-chain replacement; shorter half-life but still persistent |
| **PFBS** | C₄F₉SO₃⁻ | 299.09 | Short-chain sulfonate; faster excretion |
| **Fluoride (F⁻)** | F⁻ | 19.00 | Simple inorganic ion; completely different from organofluorine PFAS |

## Medical Information

### Origin

#### Endogenous

PFAS are **not endogenous**. They are entirely anthropogenic synthetic chemicals.

#### Exogenous

- **Drinking water:** Contaminated by industrial discharge, AFFF firefighting foam runoff. EPA MCL for PFOA and PFOS: 4 ng/L (2024 rule).
- **Food:** Migration from food packaging; bioaccumulation in fish, meat, dairy.
- **Consumer products:** Non-stick cookware, stain-resistant fabrics, cosmetics.
- **Occupational:** Manufacturing workers, firefighters (AFFF exposure).
- **Air/dust:** Indoor dust from treated textiles and carpets.

### Primary & Secondary Biological Roles

PFAS have **no physiological role**. They are xenobiotic persistent organic pollutants (POPs).

**Known toxic effects (epidemiological evidence):**
- Immunotoxicity (reduced vaccine response)
- Thyroid disruption
- Hepatotoxicity (elevated liver enzymes)
- Dyslipidaemia (elevated cholesterol)
- Developmental toxicity
- Potential carcinogenicity (kidney, testicular cancer -- IARC 2B for PFOA)

### Catabolism and Elimination Pathway

- PFAS are **not metabolised** in humans (C-F bonds are among the strongest in organic chemistry).
- Long-chain PFAS (PFOS, PFOA): half-lives of **3-8 years** in humans. Eliminated very slowly via urine, faeces, and menstrual blood.
- Short-chain PFAS (PFBS, PFHxA): half-lives of **days to weeks**; faster renal excretion.
- Primary elimination route for short-chain: **urine** (renal clearance).
- Long-chain PFAS bind strongly to serum albumin; minimal urinary excretion.

### Expression in Humans

#### Normal Levels

| Compartment | Reference Range |
|---|---|
| **Serum PFOS (general population)** | 1-20 ng/mL (declining since phase-out) |
| **Serum PFOA** | 0.5-5 ng/mL |
| **Urine PFOS** | <LOD to ~0.1 ng/mL (long-chain: very low in urine) |
| **Urine PFBA/PFBS (short-chain)** | 0.1-5 ng/mL (more readily excreted) |
| **Serum is primary matrix** | Urine is used mainly for short-chain PFAS |

#### Factors Influencing Levels

**Increased:**
- Proximity to contaminated sites (military bases, industrial facilities)
- Occupation (firefighters, fluorochemical workers)
- Consumption of contaminated water or food
- Use of PFAS-containing consumer products

**Decreased:**
- Avoidance of contaminated sources
- Time since last exposure (slow clearance)
- Cholestyramine treatment (experimental: enhances faecal excretion)

#### Associated Pathologies

| Condition | PFAS Association | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Elevated cholesterol** | PFOA/PFOS associated with dyslipidaemia | Strong epidemiological evidence |
| **Thyroid disease** | PFOS linked to thyroid disruption | Moderate evidence |
| **Immune suppression** | Reduced vaccine antibody response (children) | Strong (basis for EPA health advisory) |
| **Kidney cancer** | PFOA: IARC Group 2B | Limited human evidence |
| **Testicular cancer** | PFOA: elevated risk in exposed workers | Limited evidence |
| **Pre-eclampsia** | PFAS exposure linked to pregnancy complications | Emerging evidence |

### Presence in Urine

**Should they be normally present?** In the current environment, trace PFAS are detectable in most human urine, reflecting ubiquitous exposure.

**Normal urinary levels:** Short-chain: 0.1-5 ng/mL. Long-chain: mostly below LOD in urine (better measured in serum).

**Form in urine:** Ionised species (perfluoroalkyl sulfonates/carboxylates) in their anionic forms.

**Solubility:** PFAS are amphiphilic (surfactant-like); the ionic species are water-soluble.

## Detection in Urine

### Available Clinical Assays

1. **LC-MS/MS (gold standard):**
   - **Principle:** Online SPE or protein precipitation + reversed-phase HPLC + tandem MS in negative ESI mode.
   - **Detection:** MRM transitions (e.g., PFOS m/z 499->80; PFOA m/z 413->369; PFBS m/z 299->80).
   - **LOD:** ~0.01-0.1 ng/mL per analyte.
   - **Advantages:** Definitive; multi-analyte (20-50+ PFAS simultaneously); gold standard for biomonitoring.
   - **CDC method:** Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances in Urine and Serum by Online SPE-LC-MS/MS.

2. **UPLC-MS/MS (high-throughput):**
   - Faster chromatography; same MS detection.
   - LOD: ~0.01 ng/mL.

### Optimal Urine Type for Measurement

- **Spot urine** with creatinine or specific gravity correction.
- Urine is recommended primarily for **short-chain PFAS** (PFBA, PFBS, PFHxA) which have higher urinary concentrations.
- For **long-chain PFAS** (PFOS, PFOA), **serum/plasma** is the preferred matrix.
- **PFAS-free collection materials** essential (avoid PTFE/Teflon-containing labware).
- Store frozen at -20 C.

### Actual Gold Standard

**Online SPE-LC-MS/MS** (CDC/NHANES method). Measures 20+ PFAS simultaneously in serum and urine. LOD: 0.01-0.1 ng/mL.

### Interferences in Measurement

| Interference | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **PTFE labware contamination** | False positive | PFAS leaching from Teflon/PTFE |
| **Matrix effects (ion suppression)** | Variable | Co-eluting phospholipids and salts |
| **Background PFAS contamination** | Elevated blank | Ubiquitous laboratory contamination (solvents, tubing) |
| **Branched isomers** | Multiple peaks | Linear vs branched PFOS require careful chromatography |

### Research Detection Methods

#### Spectroscopy Detection (UV-Vis / NIR)

- PFAS have minimal UV-Vis absorption (no chromophore in most species).
- **PFOA/PFOS absorption:** Very weak at <210 nm. Not useful for direct UV detection in urine.
- NIR: No useful absorption.

#### Fluorescence Detection

- Most PFAS are **non-fluorescent**.
- **Indirect competitive immunofluorescence:** Anti-PFOS antibodies + fluorescent tracer. Ex/Em: probe-dependent. LOD: ~0.1-1 ng/mL.
- **MIP-based fluorescence quenching:** PFAS adsorption to molecularly imprinted fluorescent particles. LOD: ~1 ng/mL.
- Still research stage; not validated for clinical use.

#### Raman Detection

- PFAS have characteristic C-F stretch modes:

| Peak (cm-1) | Assignment |
|---|---|
| **~730** | CF2 symmetric stretch |
| **~1220** | CF2 asymmetric stretch |
| **~390** | CF2 wagging |

- Conventional Raman: LOD >>1 ug/mL (far too insensitive).
- SERS: LOD potentially ~1-10 ng/mL on specialised substrates. Research stage.

#### FTIR Detection

| Band (cm-1) | Assignment | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **~1150-1250** | C-F stretching (strong, broad) | Characteristic of perfluoroalkyl |
| **~1320-1370** | CF2 symmetric stretch | -- |

- ATR-FTIR can detect PFAS only at mg/L levels. Not useful for ng/L biomonitoring levels.

#### Voltammetry Detection

- PFAS are **electrochemically inert** in the normal potential window (C-F bonds extremely strong).
- **Indirect electrochemical:** PFAS-selective MIP on electrode reduces capacitance/impedance upon binding.
  - EIS-based sensors: LOD ~0.1-10 ng/mL.
- **Boron-doped diamond electrodes (BDD):** Can reduce some PFAS at very negative potentials (~-2.5 V). Research stage.

### Other Detection Technologies

1. **Total organic fluorine (TOF/EOF):** Combustion ion chromatography or extractable organic fluorine. Measures total fluorinated compounds without speciation. LOD: ~2-10 ng/mL F.
2. **Particle-induced gamma-ray emission (PIGE):** Total fluorine. LOD: ~50 ng/mL F.
3. **Immunoassay kits (lateral flow):** Emerging rapid tests for PFOS/PFOA in water. LOD: ~1-10 ng/L. Not validated for urine.
4. **Paper spray MS:** Direct ionisation from paper substrate + MS. LOD: ~0.5 ng/mL. Rapid.
