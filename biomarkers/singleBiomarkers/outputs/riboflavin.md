Riboflavin is a water-soluble B vitamin and the direct precursor of FMN and FAD, the essential flavin coenzymes. It is one of the **most intensely fluorescent** natural compounds (Ex 450 nm / Em 525 nm, quantum yield ~25%), making it the dominant contributor to the yellow-green fluorescence of urine under UV light. Excess riboflavin is rapidly excreted in urine, producing characteristically bright yellow urine.

### Molecules Not to Be Confused With

| Molecule | Formula | MW (g/mol) | Key Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| **FMN** | C₁₇H₂₁N₄O₉P | 456.34 | Phosphorylated riboflavin; similar fluorescence |
| **FAD** | C₂₇H₃₃N₉O₁₅P₂ | 785.55 | Adenylated FMN; lower quantum yield (quenched by adenine) |
| **Lumiflavin** | C₁₃H₁₂N₄O₂ | 256.26 | Photodegradation product; slightly different fluorescence |
| **Lumichrome** | C₁₂H₁₀N₄O₂ | 242.23 | Another photodegradation product |

## Medical Information

### Origin

#### Endogenous

Riboflavin cannot be synthesised by humans -- it is an **essential vitamin** obtained from diet. Some production by intestinal bacteria, but absorption from colon is minimal.

#### Exogenous

Dietary sources: dairy products (major source), eggs, lean meats, green leafy vegetables, fortified cereals and breads. RDA: 1.1 mg/day (women), 1.3 mg/day (men). Supplements often contain 25-100 mg.

### Primary & Secondary Biological Roles

**Primary role:**
- **Coenzyme precursor:** Riboflavin is converted to FMN and FAD, required by ~75 flavoproteins for redox reactions in energy metabolism, fatty acid oxidation, drug metabolism, and antioxidant defence (glutathione reductase).

**Secondary roles:**
- **Antioxidant:** Via glutathione reductase (FAD-dependent).
- **Migraine prophylaxis:** High-dose riboflavin (400 mg/day) reduces migraine frequency.
- **Neonatal jaundice phototherapy:** Riboflavin in infants' skin generates ROS under blue light.

### Catabolism and Elimination Pathway

- Absorbed in small intestine via specific riboflavin transporter (RFVT).
- Converted to FMN (riboflavin kinase) and FAD (FMN adenylyltransferase) in cells.
- Excess free riboflavin and metabolites (7-alpha-hydroxyriboflavin, lumiflavin) are excreted in urine.
- **Renal excretion** is the primary elimination route; both glomerular filtration and active tubular secretion contribute.
- Urinary riboflavin excretion increases proportionally with intake above tissue saturation.

### Expression in Humans

#### Normal Levels

| Compartment | Reference Range |
|---|---|
| **Plasma riboflavin** | 5-50 nmol/L (highly variable with intake) |
| **Erythrocyte FAD** | 300-600 nmol/L |
| **Urinary riboflavin (24-h)** | 120-400 ug/day (RDA intake); can exceed >10 mg/day with supplements |
| **EGRac (functional test)** | <1.2 normal; 1.2-1.4 marginal; >1.4 deficient |

#### Factors Influencing Levels

**Increased urinary riboflavin:**
- High dietary intake or supplementation (bright yellow urine)
- Energy drinks, B-complex vitamins
- Tissue catabolism

**Decreased urinary riboflavin:**
- Riboflavin deficiency
- Malabsorption (celiac, IBD)
- Alcoholism
- Hypothyroidism (reduced conversion to coenzymes)
- Pregnancy (increased demands)

#### Associated Pathologies

| Condition | Riboflavin Pattern | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Ariboflavinosis** | Low urinary riboflavin, high EGRac | Angular cheilitis, glossitis, seborrhoeic dermatitis, normocytic anaemia |
| **MADD (riboflavin-responsive)** | Low flavin levels | Hypoglycaemia, metabolic acidosis, myopathy |

### Presence in Urine

**Should it be normally present?** **Yes** -- riboflavin is normally excreted in urine and is the primary yellow-green fluorescent compound.

**Normal urinary levels:** 120-400 ug/day.

**Form in urine:** Free **riboflavin** molecule. Minor metabolites: 7-alpha-hydroxyriboflavin, lumiflavin.

**Solubility:** Limited (~100-130 mg/L at neutral pH) but urinary concentrations are typically well below saturation.

## Detection in Urine

### Available Clinical Assays

1. **HPLC with fluorescence detection:**
   - **Principle:** C18 reversed-phase HPLC separates riboflavin from FMN and FAD.
   - **Detection:** **Ex 450 nm / Em 520-525 nm**.
   - **LOD:** ~1-5 nmol/L (~0.4-2 ug/L).
   - **Advantages:** Gold standard; specific.

2. **Microbiological assay (Lactobacillus rhamnosus):**
   - **Principle:** Growth of bacteria proportional to riboflavin concentration.
   - **Detection:** Turbidimetric (OD 600 nm) after 16-24 h incubation.
   - **LOD:** ~1 nmol/L.
   - **Disadvantages:** Slow, labour-intensive.

### Optimal Urine Type for Measurement

- **24-hour urine** for total excretion (nutritional assessment).
- Protect from light (riboflavin is photosensitive; decomposes to lumiflavin and lumichrome).
- Store at -20 C.

### Actual Gold Standard

**HPLC-fluorescence** (Ex 450 / Em 520 nm). EGRac for functional B2 status assessment.

### Interferences in Measurement

| Interference | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Light** | Degradation | Photolysis to lumiflavin/lumichrome |
| **FMN/FAD** | Spectral overlap | Same isoalloxazine fluorophore |
| **Tetracyclines** | Fluorescence quenching | Complexation |
| **Metal ions (Cu2+, Fe3+)** | Quenching | Heavy metal quenching |

### Research Detection Methods

#### Spectroscopy Detection (UV-Vis / NIR)

- **UV-Vis:** Riboflavin absorbs at **223, 267, 373, and 450 nm**. The 450 nm band (epsilon ~11,300 M-1cm-1) gives the yellow colour. LOD: ~0.1 uM direct; ~0.01 uM with long pathlength.

#### Fluorescence Detection

- **Direct fluorescence: Ex 450 / Em 525 nm.** Most sensitive routine method. LOD: ~1 nM. Quantum yield ~25%.
- **EEM fluorescence with chemometrics:** Resolves riboflavin from FAD and other fluorophores. LOD: ~5 nM.
- **Time-resolved:** Riboflavin lifetime ~4.7 ns (distinguishable from FAD ~2.3 ns).
- **Sample prep:** Dilution; protect from light.

#### Raman Detection

| Peak (cm-1) | Assignment |
|---|---|
| **~1350** | CN stretch (isoalloxazine) |
| **~1500** | C=N stretch |
| **~1580** | C=C stretch |
| **~1630** | C=O stretch |

- Resonance Raman (Ex 458-514 nm) enhances isoalloxazine peaks. LOD: ~0.1-1 uM.
- SERS: LOD ~1 nM with Ag substrates.

#### FTIR Detection

Similar to FAD -- isoalloxazine bands at 1650, 1580, 1540 cm-1. Not practical for urinary concentrations.

#### Voltammetry Detection

- Riboflavin undergoes reversible 2e-/2H+ reduction at ~-0.4 to -0.5 V vs Ag/AgCl (pH 7).
- LOD: ~10 nM on modified electrodes (graphene, CNTs).
- Used in B2 biosensors.

### Other Detection Technologies

1. **LC-MS/MS:** LOD ~0.1 nM. Definitive identification.
2. **Paper-based fluorescence:** UV LED excitation + smartphone detection of riboflavin fluorescence on paper strip. LOD: ~50 nM. POC potential.
