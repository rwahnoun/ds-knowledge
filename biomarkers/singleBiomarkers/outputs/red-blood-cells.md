Red blood cells are the most abundant cells in blood (~4.5–5.5 million/µL). They carry haemoglobin for oxygen transport. In urine, their presence (haematuria) is always abnormal above trace quantities and indicates bleeding somewhere in the urinary tract. RBCs in urine may be **isomorphic** (uniform, suggesting lower urinary tract origin) or **dysmorphic** (distorted, acanthocytes, suggesting glomerular origin).

### Molecules Not to Be Confused With

| Entity | Description | Key Difference |
|---|---|---|
| **Free haemoglobin** | Hb released from lysed RBCs | Dissolved protein, no cells visible on microscopy; dipstick positive |
| **Myoglobin** | Muscle protein (MW 17,800 Da) | Similar dipstick reaction; distinguished by serum colour and CK levels |
| **White blood cells** | Leukocytes, 10–15 µm, granular | Larger, nucleated, different microscopic appearance |
| **Yeast cells** | *Candida*, 3–8 µm, budding | Oval, budding, no central pallor |

## Medical Information

### Origin

#### Endogenous: Erythropoiesis

RBCs are produced in the **bone marrow** (adults) from haematopoietic stem cells through erythropoiesis, stimulated by **erythropoietin (EPO)** from the kidneys. Stages: proerythroblast, basophilic, polychromatic, orthochromatic erythroblast, reticulocyte (enucleation), mature RBC. Production rate: ~200 billion RBCs/day. The process requires iron, vitamin B12, folate, and EPO.

#### Exogenous

RBCs are not obtained exogenously under normal conditions. Blood transfusion introduces donor RBCs. No dietary source of intact RBCs exists.

### Primary & Secondary Biological Roles

**Primary role:**
- **Oxygen transport:** Haemoglobin in RBCs binds O₂ in lungs and delivers it to tissues.
- **CO₂ transport:** RBCs carry ~20% of CO₂ as carbaminohaemoglobin; carbonic anhydrase converts CO₂ to HCO₃⁻.

**Secondary roles:**
- **pH buffering:** Haemoglobin acts as a buffer via histidine residues.
- **Nitric oxide metabolism:** Deoxyhaemoglobin reduces nitrite to NO, contributing to vasodilation.
- **Blood viscosity:** RBCs are the primary determinant of blood viscosity.

### Catabolism and Elimination Pathway

- Aged/damaged RBCs are removed by **reticuloendothelial macrophages** in the spleen, liver, and bone marrow.
- Haemoglobin is broken down: globin → amino acids; heme → biliverdin → bilirubin (excreted in bile); iron → recycled via transferrin.
- RBCs should **not be eliminated in urine** — their presence indicates pathology.
- Normal glomerular filtration barrier (endothelium + basement membrane + podocytes) prevents RBC passage.

### Expression in Humans

#### Normal Levels

| Compartment | Reference Range |
|---|---|
| **Blood RBC count** | Male: 4.5–5.5 × 10^12/L; Female: 4.0–5.0 × 10^12/L |
| **Urine RBCs (microscopy)** | 0–2 RBC/HPF (high-power field); <5 RBC/HPF considered normal |
| **Urine RBCs (flow cytometry)** | <25 RBC/µL (Sysmex UF) |
| **Dipstick blood** | Negative (trace may be acceptable) |

#### Factors Influencing Levels

**Increased RBCs in urine (haematuria):**
- Urinary tract infection
- Nephrolithiasis (kidney stones)
- Glomerulonephritis (IgA nephropathy, post-streptococcal)
- Bladder/renal/prostate cancer
- Trauma, vigorous exercise
- Menstrual contamination
- Anticoagulant therapy
- Polycystic kidney disease

**Decreased / absent (normal state):**
- Healthy urinary tract
- Adequate hydration
- No structural or inflammatory urinary tract pathology

#### Associated Pathologies

| Condition | RBC Pattern | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **IgA nephropathy** | Dysmorphic RBCs, RBC casts | Episodic gross haematuria with URI; most common glomerulonephritis worldwide |
| **Nephrolithiasis** | Isomorphic RBCs | Flank pain, colicky, +/- gross haematuria; prevalence ~10% lifetime |
| **Bladder cancer** | Isomorphic, painless gross haematuria | Risk factors: smoking, age >50; ~550,000 new cases/year globally |
| **UTI** | Isomorphic + bacteria + WBCs | Dysuria, frequency |
| **Exercise-induced haematuria** | Transient, resolves 24–72 h | Benign; occurs in ~20% of runners |
| **Glomerulonephritis** | Dysmorphic RBCs, acanthocytes >5%, RBC casts | Proteinuria, oedema, hypertension |

### Presence in Urine

**Should it be normally present?** **No** — only trace amounts (0–2/HPF). More than 3–5 RBC/HPF constitutes microscopic haematuria.

**Normal urinary levels:** <5 RBC/HPF on microscopy; dipstick negative.

**Form in urine:** **Intact cells** — erythrocytes in their native biconcave form (isomorphic) or distorted form (dysmorphic/acanthocytes from glomerular passage). In hypotonic or alkaline urine, RBCs may lyse → "ghost cells" releasing free haemoglobin.

**Pathological significance:**

| Urinary RBCs | Possible Causes | Prevalence |
|---|---|---|
| **Microscopic haematuria (>3 RBC/HPF)** | Stones, infection, cancer, glomerular disease, exercise | ~2–31% of general population |
| **Gross haematuria (visible)** | Cancer, stones, severe UTI, glomerulonephritis, trauma | ~0.5% of adults presenting |
| **Absent** | Normal | — |

**Solubility:** Not applicable — intact cells, not dissolved.

## Detection in Urine

### Available Clinical Assays

1. **Urine dipstick (chemical detection of haemoglobin):**
   - **Principle:** Pseudoperoxidase activity of haemoglobin catalyses oxidation of tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) by organic peroxide → green-blue colour.
   - **Detection:** Colour change on reagent pad graded 0 to 3+ (or trace to large). Detects both intact RBCs (spotted pattern) and free Hb (uniform colour).
   - **LOD:** ~5–10 RBC/µL (~150–300 µg/L free Hb); corresponds to ~5 RBC/HPF.
   - **Advantages:** Rapid (<2 min), inexpensive, high sensitivity (~91–100%).
   - **Disadvantages:** Cannot distinguish haemoglobin from myoglobin; false positives from oxidising agents.

2. **Urine microscopy (sediment analysis):**
   - **Principle:** Centrifuged urine sediment examined under microscope (400x HPF). RBCs appear as pale biconcave discs (~7 µm).
   - **Detection:** RBC count per HPF; morphology assessment (isomorphic vs dysmorphic).
   - **LOD:** ~1 RBC/HPF (practical).
   - **Advantages:** Differentiates isomorphic vs dysmorphic; identifies casts.
   - **Disadvantages:** Operator-dependent, semi-quantitative, time-consuming.

3. **Phase-contrast microscopy:**
   - **Principle:** Enhanced visualisation of RBC morphology for dysmorphic assessment (acanthocytes).
   - **Detection:** >5% acanthocytes = glomerular origin (sensitivity ~52–72%, specificity ~98%).
   - **LOD:** As for standard microscopy.

4. **Automated flow cytometry (Sysmex UF-5000):**
   - **Principle:** Fluorescent nucleic acid dye + flow cytometry; RBCs distinguished from WBCs and bacteria by scatter/fluorescence profile.
   - **Detection:** RBC/µL count.
   - **LOD:** ~5 RBC/µL.
   - **Advantages:** Rapid, reproducible, automated.
   - **Disadvantages:** Does not assess morphology; cannot distinguish dysmorphic.

### Optimal Urine Type for Measurement

- **Midstream clean-catch specimen** — standard for microscopy and dipstick.
- **First morning urine** — concentrated, but crenated/lysed RBCs may be harder to identify; no clear advantage.
- Fresh sample is critical: analyse within **1–2 hours** (RBCs lyse in hypotonic/alkaline urine).
- **Do not refrigerate** for extended periods if microscopy planned (crystal formation obscures sediment).
- Avoid menstrual contamination in women (note timing).

### Actual Gold Standard

**Manual urine microscopy** of centrifuged sediment remains the reference for RBC detection and morphology (isomorphic vs dysmorphic). Phase-contrast microscopy at 400x is the gold standard for dysmorphic RBC assessment. The dipstick is the most widely used screening tool, with microscopy for confirmation.

### Interferences in Measurement

| Interference | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Myoglobin** | False positive (dipstick) | Pseudoperoxidase activity similar to Hb |
| **Free haemoglobin (haemolysis)** | False positive for intact RBCs | Lysed RBCs release Hb; dipstick positive but no cells on microscopy |
| **Ascorbic acid (vitamin C)** | False negative (dipstick) | Reduces the chromogen, inhibiting colour development |
| **Oxidising agents (hypochlorite)** | False positive (dipstick) | Directly oxidise TMB |
| **Menstrual blood** | False positive | Contamination, not urinary bleeding |
| **Alkaline/dilute urine** | RBC lysis | Hypotonic lysis → ghost cells missed on microscopy; Hb detected by dipstick |
| **Vigorous exercise** | Transient haematuria | Benign; resolves in 24–72 h |
| **Bacterial peroxidases** | False positive (dipstick, rare) | Some bacteria produce peroxidases |

### Research Detection Methods

#### Spectroscopy Detection (UV-Vis / NIR)

- **UV-Vis spectroscopy of haemoglobin (Soret band):** Oxyhaemoglobin absorbs at **415 nm** (Soret), **542 nm**, and **577 nm** (Q bands). Methaemoglobin at 405 nm and 630 nm. Used for free Hb quantification in lysed urine.
  - LOD: ~0.5 mg/L free Hb (~10 RBC/µL if fully lysed).
  - Sample prep: Centrifuge to remove intact cells; measure supernatant.
- **NIR:** Limited utility — Hb has weak NIR absorption (overtones at ~760 nm for deoxyHb). Not validated for urine.

#### Fluorescence Detection

- **Autofluorescence:** RBCs have **weak autofluorescence** from porphyrin ring of haemoglobin (Ex 405 nm / Em 635 nm), but heavily quenched by iron. Not practically useful.
- **Fluorescent nucleic acid dyes (automated analysers):** Since mature RBCs lack nuclei, they show **no nucleic acid fluorescence** — this is how Sysmex UF differentiates RBCs from WBCs and bacteria. RBCs are identified by forward scatter (size) + side scatter + low fluorescence.
- **Zinc protoporphyrin fluorescence:** In reticulocytes/immature RBCs, ZnPP fluoresces (Ex 425 nm / Em 594 nm). Not used for urinary RBC detection.

#### Raman Detection

RBC Raman spectrum is dominated by haemoglobin:

| Peak (cm⁻¹) | Assignment |
|---|---|
| **~750** | Pyrrole breathing (porphyrin) |
| **~1003** | Phenylalanine |
| **~1223** | Amide III |
| **~1375** | Oxidation state marker (ν₄) |
| **~1550–1580** | Spin state marker (ν₂, ν₁₉) |
| **~1620–1640** | Vinyl C=C stretch |

- Resonance Raman with excitation at **532 nm** (Q-band resonance) strongly enhances porphyrin modes; single-cell Raman is feasible.
- LOD: Single cell level (confocal); ~100 RBC/µL in bulk urine.
- SERS not typically needed due to resonance enhancement.
- **Sample prep:** Centrifugation to concentrate cells; glass slide/optical window.

#### FTIR Detection

Relevant bands for RBCs/haemoglobin:

| Band (cm⁻¹) | Assignment | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **~1650** | Amide I (protein secondary structure) | α-helix dominant in Hb |
| **~1540** | Amide II | N–H bend + C–N stretch |
| **~1080** | PO₂⁻ (membrane phospholipids) | — |
| **~2850–2960** | C–H stretches (membrane lipids) | Fatty acid chains |

- ATR-FTIR on dried urine sediment can detect RBCs at ~10^3/µL. Not routinely used.
- **Sample prep:** Centrifuge urine, dry pellet on ATR crystal.

#### Voltammetry Detection

- **Haemoglobin electrochemistry:** Direct electron transfer of Hb at modified electrodes (carbon nanotubes, graphene oxide). Heme Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ redox couple at **~-0.35 V vs Ag/AgCl** (pH 7).
- LOD: ~0.1–1 mg/L free Hb in buffer.
- Not validated for intact RBC counting; detects free haemoglobin.
- **Sample prep:** Lysis to release Hb; dilution in buffer.

### Other Detection Technologies

1. **Automated image analysis (Iris iQ200, Sysmex UN-Series):** Digital microscopy with AI-based particle classification. LOD: ~5 RBC/µL. No manual operator needed.

2. **Smartphone microscopy:** Portable lens attachments + ML classification of urine sediment images. LOD: ~10–50 RBC/µL. Emerging point-of-care tool.

3. **Electrical impedance (Coulter principle):** Cell counting by impedance changes as cells pass through an aperture. Used in haematology analysers; adaptable for urine. LOD: ~5–10 cells/µL.
