
Sodium is the **principal extracellular cation** in the human body, critical for fluid balance, nerve conduction, and muscle contraction. In urine, sodium reflects dietary intake and renal handling, serving as a key diagnostic marker for volume status, AKI aetiology (FENa), and electrolyte disorders.

### Molecules Not to Be Confused With

| Molecule | Formula | MW (g/mol) | Key Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Potassium (K⁺)** | K⁺ | 39.10 | Principal intracellular cation; different renal handling |
| **Chloride (Cl⁻)** | Cl⁻ | 35.45 | Major anion; often measured alongside Na⁺; independent regulation |
| **Lithium (Li⁺)** | Li⁺ | 6.94 | Therapeutic drug for bipolar disorder; similar renal handling to Na⁺ |

## Medical Information

### Origin

#### Endogenous

Sodium is not synthesised endogenously. Total body sodium: ~60 mmol/kg body weight (~4,200 mmol in a 70 kg adult). About 40% is in bone (partially exchangeable), 55% in ECF, 5% in ICF.

#### Exogenous

Dietary sodium intake is the sole source: primarily **sodium chloride (NaCl)** in food. Typical Western diet: 100–250 mmol/day (2.3–5.8 g Na/day or 6–15 g NaCl/day). WHO recommends <2 g Na/day (<5 g NaCl/day). Sources: processed foods, added table salt, bread, cheese, cured meats.

### Primary & Secondary Biological Roles

**Primary role:**
- **Extracellular fluid osmolality regulation:** Na⁺ is the main determinant of ECF osmolality (~275–295 mOsm/kg), controlling water distribution between compartments.
- **Nerve conduction and muscle contraction:** Na⁺ influx through voltage-gated channels generates action potentials.

**Secondary roles:**
- **Acid-base balance:** Na⁺/H⁺ exchange in renal tubules contributes to acid excretion.
- **Nutrient co-transport:** Na⁺-dependent transporters (SGLT for glucose, amino acid transporters) in gut and kidney.
- **Blood pressure regulation:** Sodium balance directly affects blood volume and blood pressure.

### Catabolism and Elimination Pathway

- Na⁺ is freely filtered at the glomerulus (~25,200 mmol/day at GFR 180 L/day).
- **~99%** is reabsorbed: 67% in proximal tubule, 25% in thick ascending loop of Henle (NKCC2), 5% in distal tubule (NCC), 2–3% in collecting duct (ENaC, regulated by aldosterone).
- Final urinary excretion closely matches dietary intake (steady state).
- Minor losses: sweat (10–40 mmol/L), GI tract (<10 mmol/day normally), saliva.

### Expression in Humans

#### Normal Levels

| Compartment | Reference Range |
|---|---|
| **Serum sodium** | 135–145 mmol/L |
| **Urinary sodium (24-h)** | 40–220 mmol/day (diet-dependent) |
| **Urinary sodium (spot)** | 20–200 mmol/L (highly variable) |
| **FENa (fractional excretion)** | <1% in prerenal AKI; >2% in intrinsic renal AKI |

#### Factors Influencing Levels

**Increased urinary Na:**
- High dietary salt intake
- Diuretic therapy (loop, thiazide)
- Salt-wasting nephropathy
- Adrenal insufficiency (low aldosterone)
- SIADH (dilutional hyponatraemia with high urine Na)
- ATN / intrinsic renal failure (FENa >2%)
- Post-obstructive diuresis

**Decreased urinary Na:**
- Low salt diet
- Volume depletion / dehydration (prerenal state, FENa <1%)
- Heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome (avid Na retention)
- Hyperaldosteronism
- Pre-renal AKI

#### Associated Pathologies

| Condition | Urine Na Pattern | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Prerenal AKI (dehydration)** | UNa <20 mmol/L, FENa <1% | Oliguria, tachycardia, dry mucosa |
| **Intrinsic renal AKI (ATN)** | UNa >40 mmol/L, FENa >2% | Oliguria/anuria, rising creatinine |
| **SIADH** | UNa >30 mmol/L, dilute serum | Hyponatraemia, confusion, seizures |
| **Hyperaldosteronism** | Low UNa despite hypertension | Hypertension, hypokalaemia |
| **Salt-wasting nephropathy** | Inappropriately high UNa | Hyponatraemia, polyuria |

### Presence in Urine

**Should it be normally present?** **Yes** — sodium is normally excreted in urine, matching dietary intake. Urinary Na excretion is the primary mechanism for Na balance.

**Normal urinary levels:** 40–220 mmol/day (24-h); spot values highly variable (20–200 mmol/L).

**Form in urine:** **Na⁺ ion** — fully dissociated, hydrated monovalent cation. No crystallisation issues.

**Pathological significance:**

| Urinary Na | Possible Causes | Prevalence |
|---|---|---|
| **High (>200 mmol/day)** | High salt diet, diuretics, salt-wasting, ATN | Common |
| **Low (<20 mmol/L spot)** | Volume depletion, CHF, cirrhosis, prerenal AKI | Common |

**Solubility:** NaCl is highly soluble in water (~360 g/L at 20 C). Sodium does not form urinary crystals.

## Detection in Urine

### Available Clinical Assays

1. **Ion-selective electrode (ISE) — direct or indirect:**
   - **Principle:** Na⁺-selective glass or polymer membrane electrode generates a Nernstian potential proportional to Na⁺ activity.
   - **Detection:** Potentiometric (voltage vs reference electrode).
   - **LOD:** ~1 mmol/L.
   - **Advantages:** Standard on all automated analysers; rapid (<30 s); precise (CV <2%).
   - **Disadvantages:** Pseudohyponatraemia with indirect ISE in hyperlipidaemic/hyperproteinaemic samples (not relevant for urine).

2. **Flame photometry (historical reference):**
   - **Principle:** Na atoms in a flame emit light at **589 nm** (sodium D line, characteristic yellow emission).
   - **Detection:** Emission intensity at 589 nm proportional to Na concentration.
   - **LOD:** ~0.1 mmol/L.
   - **Advantages:** Simple, robust, multi-element (Na, K, Li simultaneously).
   - **Disadvantages:** Largely replaced by ISE; requires gas flame.

3. **Flame atomic absorption spectroscopy (FAAS):**
   - **Principle:** Ground-state Na atoms absorb 589 nm light from a sodium hollow-cathode lamp.
   - **Detection:** Absorption at **589 nm**.
   - **LOD:** ~0.01 mmol/L.
   - **Advantages:** High sensitivity; reference method.
   - **Disadvantages:** Specialised equipment.

### Optimal Urine Type for Measurement

- **24-hour urine** is preferred for assessing total daily Na excretion (dietary intake estimation, Na balance studies).
- **Spot urine** with creatinine correction (UNa/UCr ratio) is used for FENa calculation and acute clinical decisions.
- First morning urine acceptable for spot assessment.
- No special preservatives needed. Stable at room temperature for days.

### Actual Gold Standard

**Ion-selective electrode (ISE)** on automated chemistry platforms (Roche Cobas, Siemens Advia, Abbott Architect) is the universal clinical method. Direct ISE is preferred for urine. CV <2%. Reference method: flame emission photometry at 589 nm.

### Interferences in Measurement

| Interference | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Lipaemia/hyperproteinaemia** | Pseudohyponatraemia (indirect ISE only) | Excluded volume effect; not relevant for direct ISE or urine |
| **Very high K⁺** | Minor Na⁺ ISE interference | Cross-selectivity of membrane |
| **Surfactant contamination** | Variable | Alters electrode membrane response |
| **Lithium (high levels)** | Minor positive bias | Some ISE cross-react with Li⁺ |

### Research Detection Methods

#### Spectroscopy Detection (UV-Vis / NIR)

- **Flame emission (589 nm):** Classic Na detection. The sodium D-line at **589.0 and 589.6 nm** is one of the most intense atomic emission lines.
  - LOD: ~0.01 mmol/L.
  - Sample prep: Dilution in deionised water.
- **UV-Vis:** Na⁺ has no absorption in UV-Vis as a simple ion. No direct molecular spectroscopy.
- **NIR:** No useful Na⁺ absorption.

#### Fluorescence Detection

- Na⁺ itself is non-fluorescent. Indirect detection via:
- **Sodium-sensitive fluorescent probes:**
  - **SBFI (sodium-binding benzofuran isophthalate):** Ratiometric probe; Ex 340/380 nm, Em 505 nm. LOD: ~0.1 mmol/L in biological fluids. Originally for intracellular Na imaging.
  - **CoroNa Green:** Ex 492 / Em 516 nm. LOD: ~1 mmol/L. Simpler, single-wavelength.
- **Sample prep:** Mix urine with probe solution; calibrate with Na standards.
- **Limitation:** Dynamic range may not cover full urinary Na range (20–200 mmol/L) without dilution.

#### Raman Detection

Na⁺ has no Raman-active vibrational modes (monatomic ion). Raman spectroscopy cannot detect sodium.

#### FTIR Detection

Na⁺ has no infrared absorption (no molecular bonds to vibrate). FTIR cannot detect ionic sodium. Sodium salts (e.g., NaCl) show lattice modes only in far-IR (<200 cm⁻¹) in crystalline state, not useful for solution analysis.

#### Voltammetry Detection

- **Potentiometric ISE:** The standard electrochemical method (see clinical assays above). Na⁺-selective glass or polymer (bis(12-crown-4) based) membrane.
  - LOD: ~1 mmol/L.
  - Response time: <30 s.
- **Solid-state ISE on chip:** Screen-printed Na⁺ ISE for point-of-care. LOD: ~0.5 mmol/L.
- Na⁺ cannot be detected by voltammetric methods (CV, DPV) because its reduction potential is too negative (E0 = -2.71 V vs SHE) for aqueous media.

### Other Detection Technologies

1. **Inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES):** Multi-element analysis at 589 nm. LOD: ~0.001 mmol/L. Reference method for research.

2. **ICP-mass spectrometry (ICP-MS):** Ultimate sensitivity (LOD ~0.0001 mmol/L). Used for multi-element urinary panels.

3. **Paper-based ISE sensors:** Disposable Na⁺ sensors on paper substrates with ionophore membranes. LOD: ~1 mmol/L.

4. **Smartphone-based flame photometry:** Miniaturised flame emission with phone camera detection. Proof-of-concept stage.
