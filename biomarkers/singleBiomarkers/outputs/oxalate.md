# Oxalate

**Author:** Usense Healthcare

---

**Structural formula:**

```
  O=C-C=O
  |     |
  OH   OH       (oxalic acid, fully protonated)
```

Oxalate is the simplest dicarboxylic acid. At urinary pH, it exists primarily as the **oxalate dianion (C₂O₄²⁻)**. Its clinical importance lies in its propensity to form **calcium oxalate (CaOx) crystals** -- the most common component of kidney stones (~75-80% of all stones). Even small increases in urinary oxalate dramatically raise CaOx supersaturation.

### Molecules Not to Be Confused With

| Molecule | Formula | MW (g/mol) | Key Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Citrate** | C₆H₅O₇³⁻ | 189.10 | Stone inhibitor (chelates Ca); opposite role from oxalate |
| **Glycolate** | C₂H₃O₃⁻ | 75.04 | Precursor of oxalate; elevated in primary hyperoxaluria type 1 |
| **Glyoxylate** | C₂HO₃⁻ | 73.03 | Key intermediate in oxalate synthesis; substrate of AGT enzyme |

## Medical Information

### Origin

#### Endogenous

~50% of urinary oxalate is endogenously produced, primarily in the liver. Pathways: glyoxylate -> oxalate (via lactate dehydrogenase or glycolate oxidase); ascorbic acid (vitamin C) -> oxalate (non-enzymatic degradation); hydroxyproline -> oxalate.

#### Exogenous

~40-50% from dietary sources: spinach, rhubarb, beetroot, nuts, chocolate, tea, sweet potatoes. Vitamin C supplements (>1 g/day) increase oxalate via non-enzymatic degradation.

### Primary & Secondary Biological Roles

**Primary role:**
- **Metabolic waste product:** Oxalate has no known physiological function in humans. It is purely a waste product of glyoxylate and ascorbate metabolism.

**Secondary roles:**
- **Stone formation:** Calcium oxalate precipitation is the basis of most kidney stones.
- **Plant defence:** In plants, oxalate crystals deter herbivory (raphides). Not relevant to human physiology.

### Catabolism and Elimination Pathway

- Humans cannot metabolise oxalate (no oxalate-degrading enzyme, unlike gut bacteria Oxalobacter formigenes).
- **Renal excretion** is the sole elimination route: filtered at glomerulus + secreted by proximal tubule (SLC26A6 transporter).
- Intestinal Oxalobacter formigenes degrades dietary oxalate before absorption; loss of this bacterium (antibiotics) increases oxalate absorption.

### Expression in Humans

#### Normal Levels

| Compartment | Reference Range |
|---|---|
| **Plasma oxalate** | 1-5 umol/L |
| **Urinary oxalate (24-h)** | <40-45 mg/day (<0.5 mmol/day) |
| **Urinary oxalate concentration** | 10-40 mg/L |

#### Factors Influencing Levels

**Hyperoxaluria (>45 mg/day):**
- Primary hyperoxaluria (types 1, 2, 3 -- genetic; severe; oxalate >100 mg/day)
- Enteric hyperoxaluria (fat malabsorption: Crohn's, bariatric surgery)
- Dietary hyperoxaluria (high-oxalate foods, vitamin C excess)
- Ethylene glycol poisoning (metabolised to oxalate)

**Low urinary oxalate:**
- Low dietary oxalate
- Pyridoxine (B6) supplementation (cofactor for AGT, reduces glyoxylate -> oxalate)

#### Associated Pathologies

| Condition | Oxalate Pattern | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **CaOx nephrolithiasis** | Mild hyperoxaluria (40-60 mg/day) | Renal colic, haematuria; 75-80% of all stones |
| **Primary hyperoxaluria type 1** | >100 mg/day; AGT deficiency | Recurrent stones, nephrocalcinosis, ESRD; ~1:120,000 |
| **Enteric hyperoxaluria** | 60-100 mg/day | Post-bariatric surgery, Crohn's; increased stone risk |
| **Ethylene glycol poisoning** | Massive oxaluria + CaOx crystals | Metabolic acidosis, AKI, CNS depression |

### Presence in Urine

**Should it be normally present?** **Yes** -- oxalate is a normal urinary solute (10-40 mg/day).

**Normal urinary levels:** <40-45 mg/day.

**Form in urine:** **Oxalate dianion (C₂O₄²⁻)** at urinary pH >4.3. Forms insoluble calcium oxalate crystals (monohydrate: whewellite; dihydrate: weddellite) when supersaturated.

**Solubility:** Calcium oxalate is extremely insoluble (Ksp ~2.3 x 10⁻⁹). This is why even moderate increases in urinary oxalate dramatically increase stone risk. Sodium/potassium oxalate are soluble.

## Detection in Urine

### Available Clinical Assays

1. **Oxalate oxidase enzymatic assay:**
   - **Principle:** Oxalate oxidase converts oxalate + O2 -> 2CO2 + H2O2. H2O2 detected by peroxidase + chromogen (MBTH/DMAB).
   - **Detection:** 590 nm.
   - **LOD:** ~0.05 mg/dL (~5 umol/L).
   - **Advantages:** Specific for oxalate; automated.

2. **Ion chromatography:**
   - **Principle:** Anion exchange separation + conductivity detection.
   - **Detection:** Conductivity.
   - **LOD:** ~0.5 umol/L.
   - **Advantages:** Reference method; multi-anion.

3. **Capillary electrophoresis:**
   - **Principle:** Electrophoretic separation + UV detection at 200 nm.
   - **LOD:** ~1 umol/L.

### Optimal Urine Type for Measurement

- **24-hour urine** acidified with HCl (6N, ~20 mL per 24-h container) to prevent CaOx crystallisation.
- Without acidification, oxalate precipitates as CaOx crystals -> falsely low result.
- Refrigerate during collection.

### Actual Gold Standard

**Oxalate oxidase enzymatic assay** on automated platforms (Trinity Biotech, Roche). IC is the reference for research.

### Interferences in Measurement

| Interference | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Ascorbic acid** | Positive bias | Non-enzymatic conversion to oxalate during storage; also false elevation in some assays |
| **CaOx crystal precipitation** | Negative bias | Oxalate lost to crystals if urine not acidified |
| **Glycolate (high)** | Minor cross-reactivity | Some oxalate oxidase preparations |

### Research Detection Methods

#### Spectroscopy Detection (UV-Vis / NIR)

- Oxalate has weak UV absorption (<210 nm). Direct UV not practical in urine.
- Enzymatic colorimetric (590 nm): see clinical assay.
- NIR: No useful absorption.

#### Fluorescence Detection

- Oxalate is non-fluorescent. Indirect:
- **Enzymatic H2O2 -> Amplex Red (Ex 571 / Em 585 nm).** LOD: ~1 umol/L.
- **Ce(III) fluorescence quenching:** Oxalate quenches Ce3+ fluorescence (Ex 256 / Em 360 nm). LOD: ~5 umol/L.

#### Raman Detection

| Peak (cm-1) | Assignment |
|---|---|
| **~1460** | C-O symmetric stretch |
| **~1620** | C=O asymmetric stretch |
| **~890** | C-C stretch |

- Conventional Raman: LOD ~1 mM. Marginally adequate for hyperoxaluric urine.
- CaOx crystals have distinct Raman signatures used for stone composition analysis.

#### FTIR Detection

| Band (cm-1) | Assignment | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **~1620** | C=O asymmetric stretch | Strong |
| **~1320** | C-O symmetric stretch | -- |
| **~780** | O-C-O bending | -- |

- ATR-FTIR can identify CaOx crystals in urine sediment (stone analysis). Not practical for dissolved oxalate quantification.

#### Voltammetry Detection

- Oxalate is oxidised at **+0.8-1.2 V vs Ag/AgCl** on carbon or platinum electrodes.
- LOD: ~1-10 umol/L on modified electrodes (PbO2, boron-doped diamond).
- Interference from ascorbic acid at similar potentials.

### Other Detection Technologies

1. **GC-MS (after derivatisation):** LOD ~0.1 umol/L. Definitive.
2. **LC-MS/MS:** LOD ~0.5 umol/L without derivatisation.
3. **Paper-based oxalate oxidase assay:** Enzyme on paper + colour indicator. LOD: ~10 umol/L.
