# pH

**Author:** Usense Healthcare

---

## Identity Sheet

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| **Name** | Urine pH |
| **Other names** | Hydrogen ion concentration, acidity/alkalinity |
| **Definition** | pH = -log10[H+]; measure of hydrogen ion activity |
| **Nature** | Physical-chemical property (not a single molecule) |
| **Normal range** | 4.5-8.0 (typical: 5.5-6.5) |
| **Units** | pH units (dimensionless logarithmic scale) |

Urine pH reflects the kidney's role in acid-base homeostasis -- the net excretion of H+ ions (titratable acid + ammonium) vs HCO3-. Normal urine is mildly acidic (pH ~6) on a typical Western diet due to protein metabolism producing net acid. Urine pH influences the solubility and crystallisation of many substances (uric acid stones at pH <5.5, calcium phosphate stones at pH >7, struvite at pH >7.5).

### Molecules Not to Be Confused With

| Measure | Definition | Key Difference |
|---|---|---|
| **Titratable acidity** | Buffered H+ (mainly H2PO4-) | Amount of acid, not just concentration |
| **Ammonium (NH4+)** | Major urinary acid excretion vehicle | Quantitative acid excretion; not measured by pH alone |
| **Blood pH** | 7.35-7.45 | Much tighter regulation; urine pH compensates for blood pH |

## Medical Information

### Origin

#### Endogenous

Urine pH is determined by: (1) dietary acid/alkali load, (2) renal tubular H+ secretion (proximal and distal), (3) NH4+ production (proximal tubule glutaminase), (4) HCO3- reclamation, and (5) titratable acid buffering (mainly phosphate). A typical Western meat-rich diet generates ~50-100 mEq/day net acid, producing acidic urine.

#### Exogenous

Diet is the primary determinant: animal protein -> acidic urine; fruits and vegetables -> alkaline urine. Medications: potassium citrate alkalinises; ammonium chloride acidifies. Cranberry juice mildly acidifies. Sodium bicarbonate alkalinises.

### Primary & Secondary Biological Roles

**Primary role:**
- **Acid-base homeostasis:** Kidneys regulate blood pH by adjusting urinary H+ excretion. This is reflected in urine pH.

**Secondary roles:**
- **Stone chemistry determinant:** Urine pH critically affects crystal formation.
- **Drug excretion:** pH affects ionisation and tubular reabsorption of weak acids/bases (aspirin, amphetamine, methotrexate).
- **Antimicrobial:** Acidic urine inhibits bacterial growth.

### Catabolism and Elimination Pathway

Not applicable -- pH is a property, not a substance.

### Expression in Humans

#### Normal Levels

| Condition | pH Range |
|---|---|
| **Random urine** | 4.5-8.0 |
| **Typical (Western diet)** | 5.5-6.5 |
| **After meat-heavy meal** | 4.5-5.5 |
| **Vegetarian diet** | 6.5-7.5 |
| **Blood pH (for comparison)** | 7.35-7.45 |

#### Factors Influencing Levels

**Acidic urine (pH <5.5):**
- High animal protein diet
- Metabolic acidosis (DKA, lactic acidosis)
- Respiratory acidosis
- Dehydration
- Cranberry juice, ammonium chloride
- Uric acid stone formers

**Alkaline urine (pH >7.0):**
- Vegetarian/vegan diet
- Renal tubular acidosis (distal type 1 -- cannot acidify urine below 5.5)
- UTI with urease-producing organisms (Proteus splits urea -> NH3 -> alkaline)
- Metabolic/respiratory alkalosis
- Citrate/bicarbonate therapy
- Post-prandial alkaline tide

#### Associated Pathologies

| Condition | pH Pattern | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Uric acid stones** | Persistently acidic pH (<5.5) | Radiolucent stones; ~10% of all stones |
| **Distal RTA (Type 1)** | Cannot acidify <5.5 despite acidosis | CaPO4 stones, nephrocalcinosis, metabolic acidosis |
| **UTI (Proteus)** | Alkaline pH >7.5 + struvite crystals | Staghorn calculi, recurrent UTI |
| **CaPO4 stones** | Alkaline pH >6.5 | Brushite/apatite stones |

### Presence in Urine

**Should it be present?** pH is always measurable in urine.

**Normal urinary pH:** 5.5-6.5 on typical diet.

**Form:** H+ ion concentration in solution.

## Detection in Urine

### Available Clinical Assays

1. **Dipstick pH indicator:**
   - **Principle:** Double indicator system (methyl red + bromothymol blue) gives colour range from orange (pH 5) to blue (pH 9).
   - **Detection:** Visual colour comparison.
   - **Resolution:** 0.5-1.0 pH units (5.0, 5.5, 6.0, ..., 9.0).
   - **Advantages:** Rapid, included on standard dipstick.
   - **Disadvantages:** Only 0.5-unit resolution; affected by buffer overflow.

2. **Glass electrode pH meter (gold standard):**
   - **Principle:** Hydrogen-ion-selective glass membrane generates Nernstian potential vs reference electrode.
   - **Detection:** Potentiometric.
   - **Resolution:** 0.01 pH units.
   - **Advantages:** Accurate, precise, gold standard.
   - **Disadvantages:** Requires electrode maintenance; calibration.

3. **ISFET pH sensor:**
   - **Principle:** Ion-sensitive field-effect transistor with H+-sensitive gate.
   - **Resolution:** 0.01-0.1 pH units.
   - **Advantages:** Solid-state, miniaturisable, fast.

### Optimal Urine Type for Measurement

- **Fresh specimen** (CO2 loss and bacterial action alkalinise urine over time).
- Measure within **1 hour** of collection.
- First morning urine is typically most acidic (overnight acid excretion).
- For stone workup, fasting morning pH and 24-hour pH profile are useful.
- Ammonium chloride loading test for RTA diagnosis.

### Actual Gold Standard

**Glass electrode pH meter** with two-point calibration (pH 4.0 and 7.0 buffers). Resolution: 0.01 pH units.

### Interferences in Measurement

| Interference | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Delayed analysis** | Falsely alkaline | CO2 loss; bacterial urease splits urea -> NH3 |
| **Bacterial contamination** | Falsely alkaline | Urease-producing organisms |
| **Protein overflow onto pH pad** | Falsely acidic (dipstick) | Protein buffer interference |
| **Formaldehyde preservative** | Acidifies | Formic acid generation |
| **Strong buffer capacity** | Dipstick inaccurate | Overcomes indicator capacity |

### Research Detection Methods

#### Spectroscopy Detection (UV-Vis / NIR)

- **pH indicator dye spectrophotometry:** Phenol red (558 nm), bromothymol blue (616 nm), or methyl orange (507 nm) added to urine; absorbance ratio at indicator and reference wavelengths. LOD: ~0.05 pH units.
- **Fibre-optic pH sensors:** Indicator dye immobilised on fibre tip. Absorbance or fluorescence change with pH.
- **NIR:** Water O-H absorption bands shift slightly with pH; multivariate models can estimate pH. LOD: ~0.3 pH units.

#### Fluorescence Detection

- **pH-sensitive fluorophores:**
  - SNARF-1 (carboxy): ratiometric; Ex 514 / Em 580+640 nm. pKa ~7.5.
  - BCECF: ratiometric; Ex 440/490 / Em 535 nm. pKa ~6.97.
  - Fluorescein: Ex 490 / Em 520 nm. pKa ~6.4.
  - Oregon Green: Ex 496 / Em 524 nm. pKa ~4.7.
- LOD: ~0.01 pH units (ratiometric methods).
- Sample prep: Add probe or use sensor film.

#### Raman Detection

- Urine Raman spectrum changes subtly with pH (shift of phosphate, carbonate peaks).
- The H2PO4-/HPO42- ratio (peaks at 875 vs 990 cm-1) is pH-dependent (pKa 6.8).
- Not routinely used for pH measurement.

#### FTIR Detection

- Protonation state of phosphate (H2PO4- vs HPO42-) and carboxylate groups changes with pH, altering IR bands at 1080/940 cm-1.
- Multivariate FTIR models can estimate pH. LOD: ~0.2 pH units.

#### Voltammetry Detection

- **Glass electrode / ISFET:** See clinical assays -- potentiometric pH measurement is inherently electrochemical.
- **Iridium oxide pH electrode:** IrO2 thin film shows super-Nernstian response (-70 mV/pH). LOD: 0.01 pH. Robust, chip-compatible.
- **Screen-printed pH electrodes:** Disposable, low-cost. Resolution ~0.1 pH.

### Other Detection Technologies

1. **Litmus / universal indicator paper:** Colour change strips. Resolution: ~1 pH unit.
2. **Smartphone-based pH indicator reading:** Camera + app analyses colour of indicator pad/paper. Resolution: ~0.3 pH units.
3. **Optical pH sensors (optodes):** pH-sensitive dye embedded in polymer film. Resolution: 0.01 pH.
