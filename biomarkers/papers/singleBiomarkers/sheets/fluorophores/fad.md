# [[fad|FAD]] (Flavin Adenine Dinucleotide)

[[fad|FAD]] is a redox-active coenzyme derived from riboflavin (vitamin B2). The **oxidised form ([[fad|FAD]])** is fluorescent (Ex 450 nm / Em 525 nm), while the reduced form (FADH₂) is not. [[fad|FAD]] is a prosthetic group for flavoproteins involved in the electron transport chain (Complex II), fatty acid oxidation, and amino acid metabolism. In urine, [[fad|FAD]] and its precursor riboflavin contribute to the characteristic yellow-green fluorescence. See [[optical-properties]] for fluorescence characteristics and [[signatures]] for spectral data.

## Medical Information

### Origin

#### Endogenous

[[fad|FAD]] is synthesised from riboflavin (vitamin B2) in two steps: riboflavin → FMN (riboflavin kinase) → [[fad|FAD]] ([[fad|FAD]] synthetase/FMN adenylyltransferase). Most [[fad|FAD]] is tightly bound to flavoproteins (~75 flavoproteins in humans). Free [[fad|FAD]] concentration in cells is very low.

#### Exogenous

[[fad|FAD]] is not obtained directly from diet. Its precursor, **riboflavin (B2)**, is the dietary source: dairy, eggs, lean meats, green vegetables, fortified cereals. RDA: 1.1–1.3 mg/day.

### Primary & Secondary Biological Roles

**Primary role:**
- **Electron carrier:** [[fad|FAD]]/FADH₂ is a prosthetic group for succinate dehydrogenase (Complex II), acyl-CoA dehydrogenases, xanthine oxidase, monoamine oxidase, D-amino acid oxidase, and ~75 other flavoproteins.

**Secondary roles:**
- **Antioxidant systems:** Glutathione reductase requires [[fad|FAD]].
- **Metabolic imaging marker:** [[fad|FAD]]/[[nadh|NADH]] fluorescence ratio ("optical redox ratio") used in research for tissue metabolic state assessment.

### Catabolism and Elimination Pathway

- [[fad|FAD]]-bound flavoproteins are degraded by lysosomal proteases; [[fad|FAD]] is hydrolysed to FMN and then riboflavin.
- Riboflavin is the primary urinary excretion product (freely filtered, partially reabsorbed via tubular secretion).
- Urinary riboflavin (not [[fad|FAD]] per se) is the dominant yellow-green fluorescent species.
- Excess riboflavin from diet/supplements is rapidly excreted, producing bright yellow urine.

### Expression in Humans

#### Normal Levels

| Compartment | Reference Range |
|---|---|
| **Plasma riboflavin** | 5–50 nmol/L |
| **Erythrocyte [[fad\|FAD]]** | 300–600 nmol/L (functional status marker) |
| **Urinary riboflavin** | 120–400 µg/day (diet-dependent) |
| **Urinary [[fad\|FAD]]** | Trace (most excreted as riboflavin, not intact [[fad\|FAD]]) |

#### Factors Influencing Levels

**Increased urinary riboflavin/[[fad|FAD]] fluorescence:**
- Riboflavin supplements (bright yellow urine)
- High B2 dietary intake
- B-complex vitamins
- Increased protein catabolism

**Decreased:**
- Riboflavin deficiency (ariboflavinosis)
- Malabsorption
- Alcoholism

#### Associated Pathologies

| Condition | [[fad\|FAD]]/Riboflavin Pattern | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Ariboflavinosis (B2 deficiency)** | Low urinary riboflavin, low erythrocyte [[fad\|FAD]] | Angular cheilitis, glossitis, anaemia |
| **Multiple acyl-CoA dehydrogenase deficiency (MADD)** | [[fad\|FAD]]-responsive variant | Metabolic acidosis, hypoglycaemia, myopathy |

### Presence in Urine

**Should it be normally present?** **Yes** — riboflavin (B2) is normally excreted in urine. Its fluorescence (and that of minor FMN/[[fad|FAD]]) gives urine its yellow-green fluorescence under UV light.

**Normal urinary levels:** Riboflavin: 120–400 µg/day. Intact [[fad|FAD]]: trace amounts.

**Form in urine:** Primarily as free **riboflavin**; minor amounts of FMN and [[fad|FAD]].

**Solubility:** Freely soluble.

## Detection in Urine

### Available Clinical Assays

1. **HPLC with fluorescence detection:**
   - **Principle:** Reversed-phase HPLC separates riboflavin, FMN, and [[fad|FAD]].
   - **Detection:** Fluorescence at **Ex 450 nm / Em 520–525 nm**.
   - **LOD:** ~1–5 nmol/L.
   - **Advantages:** Specific, separates [[fad|FAD]] from riboflavin and FMN.

2. **Erythrocyte glutathione reductase activity coefficient (EGRac):**
   - **Principle:** Functional test for riboflavin/[[fad|FAD]] status; ratio of enzyme activity with/without added [[fad|FAD]].
   - **Detection:** NADPH consumption at 340 nm.
   - **EGRac >1.4** indicates deficiency.
   - **Not a direct urine assay** but the clinical gold standard for B2 status.

### Optimal Urine Type for Measurement

- **24-hour urine** for total riboflavin excretion.
- Protect from light (riboflavin is photolabile).
- Store at 4 °C or freeze.

### Actual Gold Standard

**HPLC-fluorescence (Ex 450 / Em 520 nm)** for urinary riboflavin/[[fad|FAD]]. EGRac for functional status.

### Interferences in Measurement

| Interference | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Light exposure** | False decrease | Photodecomposition of flavins |
| **Tetracyclines** | Fluorescence interference | Similar emission wavelength |
| **Other urinary fluorophores** | Spectral overlap | [[total-urinary-porphyrin\|Porphyrins]], 4-pyridoxic acid |

### Research Detection Methods

#### Spectroscopy Detection (UV-Vis / NIR)

- **UV-Vis:** [[fad|FAD]] and riboflavin absorb at **267 nm** (ε ~32,000), **373 nm** (ε ~9,300), and **450 nm** (ε ~11,300 M⁻¹cm⁻¹). The 450 nm band gives the yellow colour.
  - LOD: ~0.1 µM by direct UV-Vis.

#### Fluorescence Detection

This is the definitive detection modality for [[fad|FAD]]/riboflavin. See [[optical-properties]] for excitation-emission maps.

- **Direct fluorescence:** **Ex 450 nm / Em 520–530 nm** (green fluorescence). Quantum yield ~25% (riboflavin), ~3% ([[fad|FAD]], quenched by adenine).
  - LOD: ~1 nM (riboflavin), ~10 nM ([[fad|FAD]]).
  - Urine can be measured directly with appropriate dilution.
- **Excitation-emission matrix (EEM) fluorescence:** Simultaneous multi-excitation scanning to resolve [[fad|FAD]], riboflavin, FMN, and other fluorophores.
  - LOD: ~5 nM with chemometric deconvolution.
- **Time-resolved fluorescence:** [[fad|FAD]] lifetime (~2.3 ns free, ~0.3 ns quenched in proteins) distinguishes it from riboflavin (~4.7 ns).

#### Raman Detection

| Peak (cm⁻¹) | Assignment |
|---|---|
| **~1350** | Isoalloxazine ring (CN stretch) |
| **~1500** | Isoalloxazine C=N stretch |
| **~1580** | Isoalloxazine C=C stretch |
| **~1630** | C=O stretch |

- Conventional Raman: LOD ~100 µM.
- **Resonance Raman (Ex 458/514 nm):** Enhanced by factor of ~10³ due to isoalloxazine absorption. LOD: ~0.1–1 µM.
- SERS: LOD ~1–10 nM with Ag substrates.

#### FTIR Detection

| Band (cm⁻¹) | Assignment | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **~1650** | C=O stretch | Isoalloxazine |
| **~1580** | C=C/C=N stretch | Ring |
| **~1540** | NH bending | Amide |
| **~1080** | PO₄ stretch | Phosphodiester |

- Not practical for urinary [[fad|FAD]] detection (trace levels).

#### Voltammetry Detection

- [[fad|FAD]] undergoes two-electron two-proton reduction: [[fad|FAD]] + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → FADH₂.
- **Reduction potential:** ~-0.22 V vs SHE at pH 7 (~-0.46 V vs Ag/AgCl).
- LOD: ~0.1 µM on carbon electrodes.
- Used in biosensor design ([[fad|FAD]]-mediated [[glucose]] oxidase sensors).

### Other Detection Technologies

1. **LC-MS/MS:** Definitive identification. LOD: ~0.1 nM. Sample prep: protein precipitation.
2. **Microbiological assay (Lactobacillus rhamnosus growth):** Traditional B2 assay. LOD: ~1 nM. Time: 16–24 h.

## Gaps

- Intact urinary [[fad|FAD]] vs riboflavin vs FMN fractionation is rarely performed; most studies report total flavin fluorescence.
- EEM-based deconvolution of [[fad|FAD]] from other urinary fluorophores ([[tryptophan]] metabolites, [[total-urinary-porphyrin|porphyrins]], pyridoxic acid) in complex urine matrices is not fully validated.
- The [[fad|FAD]]/[[nadh|NADH]] optical redox ratio as a urine biomarker for systemic metabolic state has not been clinically validated.
- Photostability of urinary flavins during sample handling and automated analysis is not standardised.
