# Haemoglobin

Haemoglobin is a **globular metalloprotein** (~64.5 kDa) composed of four globin subunits, each containing one heme prosthetic group. The heme iron (Fe²⁺) reversibly binds oxygen. Haemoglobin is normally **intracellular** within [[red-blood-cells|RBC]]. Its presence as a free molecule in urine (haemoglobinuria) indicates intravascular haemolysis or [[red-blood-cells|RBC]] lysis within the urinary tract. It has an intense **Soret absorption band at ~415 nm** and Q bands at 540 and 577 nm (oxyHb). See [[optical-properties]] for absorption spectra and [[signatures]] for Raman data.

## Medical Information

### Origin

#### Endogenous: Biosynthesis — Erythropoiesis

Haemoglobin is synthesised in **erythroid precursors** in the bone marrow during erythropoiesis. Globin chains (α, β) are synthesised on ribosomes. Heme is synthesised via the heme biosynthetic pathway (see Porphobilinogen sheet). Iron is inserted into protoporphyrin IX by ferrochelatase. Each [[red-blood-cells|RBC]] contains ~270 million Hb molecules. Total body Hb: ~750 g in adults. Daily Hb synthesis: ~6 g/day (replacing ~0.8% of circulating [[red-blood-cells|RBC]]).

#### Exogenous

No dietary source of intact haemoglobin contributes to circulating Hb. Dietary iron (heme iron from meat, non-heme from plants) is a critical raw material. Blood transfusion introduces donor Hb/[[red-blood-cells|RBC]]. Recombinant Hb-based oxygen carriers (HBOCs) are experimental.

### Primary & Secondary Biological Roles

**Primary role:**
- **Oxygen transport:** Hb carries O₂ from lungs to tissues (cooperative binding, sigmoid O₂ dissociation curve, P50 ~26 mmHg).

**Secondary roles:**
- **CO₂ transport:** ~15–20% of CO₂ carried as carbaminohaemoglobin.
- **pH buffering:** Histidine residues buffer H⁺; Bohr effect links pH to O₂ release.
- **Nitric oxide (NO) processing:** DeoxyHb reduces nitrite to NO (vasodilator); oxyHb scavenges NO.

### Catabolism and Elimination Pathway

- Aged [[red-blood-cells|RBC]] are phagocytosed by splenic/hepatic macrophages.
- Hb → globin (recycled to amino acids) + heme → biliverdin → **bilirubin** (conjugated in liver, excreted in bile).
- Iron is recycled via ferritin/transferrin.
- Free plasma Hb (from intravascular haemolysis) is bound by **haptoglobin** → cleared by hepatic macrophages.
- When haptoglobin is saturated, free Hb is **filtered by the glomerulus** (Hb dimers ~32 kDa pass the glomerular barrier). Tubular cells reabsorb Hb (megalin/cubilin receptors) and catabolise it.
- When reabsorptive capacity is exceeded → **haemoglobinuria** (free Hb in urine).

### Expression in Humans

#### Normal Levels

| Compartment | Reference Range |
|---|---|
| **Blood Hb** | Male: 13.5–17.5 g/dL; Female: 12.0–16.0 g/dL |
| **Plasma free Hb** | <5 mg/dL (normally negligible) |
| **Urine Hb (free)** | Absent (negative on dipstick) |
| **Urine [[red-blood-cells\|RBC]]/Hb (dipstick)** | Negative |

#### Factors Influencing Levels

**Increased Hb in urine (haemoglobinuria):**
- Intravascular haemolysis (autoimmune haemolytic anaemia, PNH, TTP, HUS, malaria)
- Transfusion reactions (ABO incompatibility)
- Mechanical haemolysis (prosthetic heart valves, march haemoglobinuria)
- Severe burns
- G6PD deficiency (drug/food-triggered haemolysis)
- Paroxysmal cold haemoglobinuria
- In-vitro [[red-blood-cells|RBC]] lysis in urine (alkaline/hypotonic urine)

**Absent (normal):**
- Intact [[red-blood-cells|RBC]] membranes, functional haptoglobin system
- No haemolytic process

#### Associated Pathologies

| Condition | Urine Hb | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria (PNH)** | Dark morning urine, free Hb | Haemolysis, thrombosis, pancytopenia; rare (~1–10/million) |
| **Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia** | Haemoglobinuria if severe | Anaemia, jaundice, fatigue |
| **Transfusion reaction** | Acute haemoglobinuria | Fever, flank pain, dark urine, DIC |
| **Rhabdomyolysis (myoglobin)** | Dipstick positive but myoglobin not Hb | Muscle pain, dark urine, AKI risk |
| **March haemoglobinuria** | Transient, exercise-related | Benign; after prolonged marching/running |

### Presence in Urine

**Should it be normally present?** **No** — urine should be free of haemoglobin. Any detection indicates intravascular haemolysis (free Hb) or [[red-blood-cells|RBC]] lysis within the urinary tract.

**Normal urinary levels:** Undetectable (negative dipstick, <0.03 mg/dL).

**Form in urine:** Free haemoglobin is present as **Hb dimers** (~32 kDa, αβ pairs) or tetramers; also oxidised forms: **methaemoglobin** (Fe³⁺). In aged samples, Hb degrades to heme and globin.

**Pathological significance:**

| Urinary Hb | Possible Causes | Prevalence |
|---|---|---|
| **Present (haemoglobinuria)** | Intravascular haemolysis, severe haematuria with [[red-blood-cells\|RBC]] lysis | Variable; PNH ~1:100,000 |
| **Absent** | Normal | — |

**Solubility:** Haemoglobin is soluble in water/urine at physiological concentrations. At very high concentrations, Hb can precipitate in renal tubules (Hb cast nephropathy), especially at acidic pH.

## Detection in Urine

### Available Clinical Assays

1. **Urine dipstick (pseudoperoxidase reaction):**
   - **Principle:** Hb (and myoglobin) catalyses oxidation of TMB (3,3',5,5'-tetramethylbenzidine) by organic peroxide → green-blue colour.
   - **Detection:** Colour change graded trace to 3+. Intact [[red-blood-cells|RBC]] produce spotted pattern; free Hb produces uniform colour.
   - **LOD:** ~0.015–0.062 mg/dL free Hb (~5–10 intact [[red-blood-cells|RBC]]/µL).
   - **Absorption:** TMB oxidised product absorbs at ~660 nm.
   - **Advantages:** Rapid, inexpensive, very sensitive.
   - **Disadvantages:** Does not distinguish Hb from myoglobin. False positives from oxidants.

2. **Spectrophotometry (Soret band):**
   - **Principle:** Direct UV-Vis measurement of free Hb in centrifuged urine supernatant.
   - **Detection:** Absorbance at **415 nm** (Soret band).
   - **LOD:** ~0.5 mg/dL.
   - **Advantages:** Quantitative, specific for Hb (vs myoglobin at 409 nm).
   - **Disadvantages:** Requires spectrophotometer; not point-of-care.

3. **Immunoturbidimetry / immunonephelometry:**
   - **Principle:** Anti-Hb antibodies cause agglutination proportional to Hb concentration.
   - **Detection:** Turbidity at 340 nm.
   - **LOD:** ~0.01 mg/dL.
   - **Advantages:** Specific for Hb (not myoglobin).
   - **Disadvantages:** Costly, specialised.

### Optimal Urine Type for Measurement

- **Fresh midstream urine** — [[red-blood-cells|RBC]] lyse over time in dilute/alkaline urine, converting haematuria to haemoglobinuria.
- Analyse within **1–2 hours** of collection.
- **First morning urine** is more concentrated but may show more [[red-blood-cells|RBC]] lysis.
- Centrifuge to distinguish: **pink supernatant** = free Hb; **clear supernatant + red pellet** = intact [[red-blood-cells|RBC]].
- Protect from light (methaemoglobin formation).

### Actual Gold Standard

The **urine dipstick** is the primary screening tool. For confirmation and quantification of free Hb, **UV-Vis spectrophotometry at 415 nm** (Soret band) on centrifuged supernatant is the reference method. The Harboe method (1959) measures Hb at 415 nm with correction for turbidity at 380 and 450 nm. Immunoassays provide the highest specificity (Hb vs myoglobin).

### Interferences in Measurement

| Interference | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Myoglobin** | False positive (dipstick) | Same pseudoperoxidase activity; MW 17.8 kDa also passes glomerulus |
| **Ascorbic acid** | False negative | Reduces oxidised TMB chromogen |
| **Hypochlorite (bleach)** | False positive | Chemical oxidation of TMB |
| **Bacterial peroxidases** | False positive (rare) | Mimic pseudoperoxidase activity |
| **High-dose captopril** | False negative | Thiol groups interfere |
| **Formaldehyde (preservative)** | False negative | Cross-links Hb, reduces peroxidase activity |
| **High specific gravity** | Decreased sensitivity | Concentrated urine inhibits chromogen development |
| **Delay in analysis** | Hb degradation | MetHb and heme formation reduce peroxidase activity |

### Research Detection Methods

#### Spectroscopy Detection (UV-Vis / NIR)

- **UV-Vis (Soret band):** OxyHb: **415 nm** (ε ≈ 131,000 M⁻¹cm⁻¹ per heme), 542 nm, 577 nm. DeoxyHb: 430 nm, 555 nm. MetHb: 405 nm, 500 nm, 630 nm.
  - LOD: ~0.1 mg/dL (~60 nM tetramer).
  - Sample prep: Centrifuge; measure supernatant; correct for turbidity.
- **NIR:** Weak absorption at ~760 nm (deoxyHb), ~940 nm (water interference). Not practical for urine. LOD: >10 mg/dL.

#### Fluorescence Detection

- **Intrinsic porphyrin fluorescence:** Hb fluorescence is heavily **quenched by iron** — very weak (quantum yield <0.01%).
- **Zinc protoporphyrin (ZnPP):** If heme iron is replaced by zinc (iron deficiency), ZnPP fluoresces strongly (Ex 425 nm / Em 594 nm). Not relevant for urinary Hb.
- **Indirect methods:** Heme extraction → demetallation → free protoporphyrin fluorescence (Ex 405 nm / Em 630 nm). LOD: ~10 nM heme.
- **Fluorescent anti-Hb immunoassays:** FITC-labelled antibodies; Ex 490 / Em 520 nm. LOD: ~0.001 mg/dL.

#### Raman Detection

Haemoglobin has a rich resonance Raman spectrum. See [[signatures]] for full assignments.

| Peak (cm⁻¹) | Assignment |
|---|---|
| **~675** | Pyrrole deformation (ν₇) |
| **~750** | Pyrrole breathing |
| **~1003** | Phenylalanine |
| **~1130** | Methine C–H deformation |
| **~1375** | Oxidation state marker (ν₄) — Fe²⁺ vs Fe³⁺ |
| **~1550–1580** | Spin/ligation state marker (ν₂) |
| **~1620** | Vinyl C=C stretch |
| **~1640** | Oxygenation marker (ν₁₀) |

- **Resonance Raman** with excitation at **405 nm (Soret)** or **532 nm (Q-band)**: enhancement factor 10³–10⁶ for porphyrin modes.
- LOD: ~1 µM Hb in buffer; ~10 µM in urine (with background subtraction).
- Can distinguish oxyHb, deoxyHb, metHb by oxidation/spin markers.

#### FTIR Detection

| Band (cm⁻¹) | Assignment | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **~1650** | Amide I (α-helix of globin) | Dominant protein secondary structure band |
| **~1540** | Amide II | N–H bend + C–N stretch |
| **~1450** | CH₂/CH₃ deformation | Side chains |
| **~1230–1240** | Amide III | — |
| **~1080** | C–O stretch (carbohydrate, if glycated) | Relevant for HbA1c |

- ATR-FTIR can detect Hb at >1 mg/dL concentrations in dried samples. Not sensitive enough for typical haemoglobinuria levels.
- LOD: ~1 mg/dL (~0.6 µM tetramer).

#### Voltammetry Detection

- **Heme iron electrochemistry:** Direct electron transfer at modified electrodes. Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺ reduction at **-0.2 to -0.4 V vs Ag/AgCl** (pH 7, carbon nanotube/graphene electrodes).
- **Amperometric detection:** Applied potential at -0.3 V; current proportional to Hb concentration.
- LOD: ~0.01–0.1 mg/dL on nanostructured electrodes.

### Other Detection Technologies

1. **Lateral flow immunoassay:** Anti-Hb antibody on nitrocellulose strip with gold nanoparticle label. LOD: ~0.01 mg/dL. Distinguishes Hb from myoglobin. 10 min. Point-of-care.

2. **Smartphone-based colorimetry:** Image analysis of dipstick colour using phone camera + app. LOD: comparable to visual dipstick (~0.015 mg/dL). Improved objectivity.

3. **Microfluidic Hb sensors:** Lab-on-chip with integrated Soret-band absorbance measurement (415 nm LED + photodiode). LOD: ~0.1 mg/dL. µL sample volumes.

## Gaps

- The dipstick cannot distinguish Hb from myoglobin; clinical differentiation still requires immunoassay or centrifugation + visual inspection, not automated.
- Resonance Raman LOD in urine matrix (not buffer) with background subtraction at clinically relevant Hb concentrations needs further validation.
- The impact of urine pH and age on Hb spectral properties (Soret shift, MetHb formation) is not well-characterised for clinical measurement contexts.
- Microfluidic Soret-band sensors have not been validated against clinical dipstick in multicentre studies.
