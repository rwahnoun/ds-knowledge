# [[nadh|NADH]] (Reduced Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide)

[[nadh|NADH]] is the reduced form of NAD‚Āļ, the central electron carrier in cellular metabolism. [[nadh|NADH]] is **intrinsically fluorescent** (Ex 340 nm / Em 460 nm), while NAD‚Āļ is not, making [[nadh|NADH]] a natural endogenous fluorophore. In the urinary context, [[nadh|NADH]] fluorescence has been explored as a marker of cellular metabolic activity and as a component of the overall urine autofluorescence spectrum. It is a research biomarker, not a routine clinical analyte. See [[optical-properties]] for fluorescence data and [[signatures]] for spectral assignments.

## Medical Information

### Origin

#### Endogenous: Cellular Metabolism

[[nadh|NADH]] is produced in all cells via glycolysis (cytoplasm), pyruvate dehydrogenase, and the TCA cycle (mitochondria). It is the primary electron donor to Complex I of the electron transport chain. Intracellular [[nadh|NADH]] concentrations: ~100‚Äď400 ¬ĶM. [[nadh|NADH]] is predominantly intracellular; extracellular/urinary levels reflect cell lysis, metabolic overflow, and renal tubular cell turnover.

#### Exogenous

[[nadh|NADH]] supplements are available commercially. Dietary NAD‚Āļ precursors include niacin (vitamin B3) and nicotinamide riboside (NR), but these do not directly contribute urinary [[nadh|NADH]].

### Primary & Secondary Biological Roles

**Primary role:**
- **Electron carrier in catabolism:** [[nadh|NADH]] donates electrons to the mitochondrial ETC for ATP production (2.5 ATP per [[nadh|NADH]]).

**Secondary roles:**
- **Redox signalling:** NAD‚Āļ/[[nadh|NADH]] ratio regulates sirtuins, PARPs, and metabolic gene expression.
- **Autofluorescence biomarker:** [[nadh|NADH]] fluorescence is used in tissue metabolic imaging (optical biopsy).

### Catabolism and Elimination Pathway

- [[nadh|NADH]] is rapidly oxidised to NAD‚Āļ in mitochondria (ETC) or cytoplasm (lactate dehydrogenase).
- Free [[nadh|NADH]] is unstable in acidic conditions (t¬Ĺ ~30 min at pH 4).
- Urinary [[nadh|NADH]] derives from cellular debris, lysed cells, and renal tubular cell turnover.
- NAD‚Āļ and its metabolites (nicotinamide, NMN, ADPR) are also excreted.

### Expression in Humans

#### Normal Levels

| Compartment | Reference Range |
|---|---|
| **Intracellular [[nadh\|NADH]]** | 100‚Äď400 ¬ĶM |
| **Blood NAD‚Āļ total** | ~20‚Äď40 ¬ĶM |
| **Urine [[nadh\|NADH]]** | Trace (not routinely measured); detectable by fluorescence |

#### Factors Influencing Levels

**Increased urinary [[nadh|NADH]] fluorescence:**
- Increased cellular turnover or damage
- UTI (bacterial metabolic activity)
- Cancer cells (altered metabolism, Warburg effect)
- Mitochondrial disorders

**Decreased:**
- Not clinically significant in isolation.

#### Associated Pathologies

[[nadh|NADH]] itself is not a standalone clinical biomarker. Its fluorescence contributes to urinary autofluorescence patterns studied in research contexts.

| Condition | [[nadh\|NADH]] Relevance | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Bladder cancer screening** | Altered [[nadh\|NADH]]/[[fad\|FAD]] ratio in exfoliated cells | Research stage |
| **UTI** | Bacterial [[nadh\|NADH]] fluorescence | Experimental |
| **Metabolic disorders** | Altered redox state | Research stage |

### Presence in Urine

**Should it be normally present?** In **trace amounts** ‚ÄĒ from normal cellular turnover. Not routinely measured.

**Normal urinary levels:** Not standardised. Detectable as fluorescence signal at Ex 340 / Em 460 nm.

**Form in urine:** Free [[nadh|NADH]] molecule (unstable, degrades in acidic urine).

**Solubility:** Freely soluble.

## Detection in Urine

> [!NOTE]
> No routine clinical assays measure urinary [[nadh|NADH]]. It is a research biomarker detected by autofluorescence methods.

### Optimal Urine Type for Measurement

- Fresh specimen required ([[nadh|NADH]] is unstable). Analyse immediately.
- Protect from light and heat.
- Neutral or slightly alkaline pH preserves [[nadh|NADH]] stability.

### Actual Gold Standard

No established clinical gold standard. **HPLC-UV at 340 nm** or **HPLC-fluorescence (Ex 340 / Em 460 nm)** are research reference methods.

### Interferences in Measurement

| Interference | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Acidic pH** | Degradation | [[nadh\|NADH]] decomposes at pH <6 |
| **Light** | Photodegradation | UV-sensitive |
| **Other fluorophores** | Spectral overlap | Riboflavin, [[fad\|FAD]], proteins fluoresce at nearby wavelengths |
| **Oxidation** | Signal loss | [[nadh\|NADH]] ‚Üí NAD‚Āļ (non-fluorescent) |

### Research Detection Methods

#### Spectroscopy Detection (UV-Vis / NIR)

- **UV absorption:** [[nadh|NADH]] absorbs at **340 nm** (őĶ ~6,220 M‚ĀĽ¬Ļcm‚ĀĽ¬Ļ). NAD‚Āļ absorbs at 260 nm but not 340 nm. The 340 nm band is diagnostic for the reduced form.
  - LOD: ~1 ¬ĶM by direct UV.
  - Sample prep: None (direct measurement of fresh urine).

#### Fluorescence Detection

This is the primary detection modality for [[nadh|NADH]]. See [[optical-properties]] for comparison with other urinary fluorophores.

- **Intrinsic fluorescence:** **Ex 340 nm / Em 460 nm** (blue fluorescence). Quantum yield ~2%.
  - LOD: ~0.1‚Äď1 ¬ĶM in buffer; ~5‚Äď10 ¬ĶM in urine (matrix quenching).
  - Lifetime: ~0.4 ns (free [[nadh|NADH]]), ~1‚Äď3 ns (protein-bound [[nadh|NADH]]).
- **Two-photon excitation:** Ex 720‚Äď740 nm (two-photon) / Em 460 nm. Used in tissue imaging.
- **FLIM (Fluorescence Lifetime Imaging):** Distinguishes free vs bound [[nadh|NADH]] by lifetime. Not applicable for bulk urine.

#### Raman Detection

| Peak (cm‚ĀĽ¬Ļ) | Assignment |
|---|---|
| **~1030** | Nicotinamide ring C-H stretch |
| **~1340** | Adenine ring |
| **~1580** | Nicotinamide ring C=C/C=N |
| **~1690** | C=O stretch (amide) |

- Conventional Raman: LOD ~1 mM (too high for urinary levels).
- SERS: Potentially LOD ~1‚Äď10 ¬ĶM. Few studies on urinary [[nadh|NADH]] specifically.

#### FTIR Detection

| Band (cm‚ĀĽ¬Ļ) | Assignment | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **~1690** | Amide C=O | Nicotinamide |
| **~1580** | Ring C=C | ‚ÄĒ |
| **~1080** | PO‚āĄ stretch | Phosphodiester |

- Not practical for urinary [[nadh|NADH]] (too low concentration, overlapping bands).

#### Voltammetry Detection

- [[nadh|NADH]] is electrochemically oxidised: [[nadh|NADH]] ‚Üí NAD‚Āļ + H‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ
- **Oxidation potential:** +0.5‚Äď0.7 V vs Ag/AgCl on bare carbon; reduced to +0.1‚Äď0.3 V on modified electrodes (Meldola Blue, carbon nanotubes, graphene).
- LOD: ~0.1‚Äď1 ¬ĶM on nanostructured electrodes.
- **Interference:** Ascorbic acid, [[uric-acid|uric acid]] oxidise at similar potentials.

### Other Detection Technologies

1. **Enzymatic cycling assays:** NAD‚Āļ/[[nadh|NADH]] quantified by lactate dehydrogenase cycling with colorimetric or fluorimetric endpoint. LOD: ~10 nM. Measures total NAD pool.
2. **LC-MS/MS:** Definitive identification and quantification. LOD: ~1 nM. Sample prep: protein precipitation, extraction.

## Gaps

- Urinary [[nadh|NADH]] concentrations in health and disease have not been systematically characterised; reference ranges do not exist.
- EEM-based separation of [[nadh|NADH]] fluorescence from overlapping signals ([[tryptophan]], tyrosine, [[fad|FAD]], riboflavin) in urine requires validated deconvolution algorithms.
- The [[nadh|NADH]]/[[fad|FAD]] autofluorescence ratio as a urinary biomarker for cancer or metabolic disease has only been demonstrated in cell culture and ex vivo tissue, not in bulk urine.
- [[nadh|NADH]] instability in urine (acidic pH, light exposure) limits sample handling flexibility in clinical research.
